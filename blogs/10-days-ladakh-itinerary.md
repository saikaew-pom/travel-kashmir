# 10 Days in Ladakh | The Road Less Travelled Itinerary

The first Ladakh trip changes you. The second one takes you further in.

Most travellers do Leh, Nubra, and Pangong — and they are right to. That circuit is spectacular. But it is also the one every tour operator offers, the one every travel blog describes, the one the roads are congested with in July and August.

This itinerary builds on that foundation and goes beyond it. It adds the 11th-century frescoes of Alchi, the lunar landscape of Lamayuru, the flamingo-visited stillness of Tso Moriri Lake — places that require more time to reach and reward that time generously. The traveller who follows this route will see Ladakh's breadth, not just its highlights.

Ten days is the right length for Ladakh. Not too compressed to enjoy, not so long that you're filling time. Every day on this itinerary earns its place.

## Day 1: Arrive Leh — First Hours at 3,524m

**Altitude:** 3,524m | **Activity level:** Rest | **Distance driven:** 0km

Your body does not know it yet, but it is working harder than usual.

At 3,524 metres, the air carries roughly 65% of the oxygen available at sea level. Red blood cells multiply over the coming days to compensate. Until they do, you will feel the difference — sometimes sharply.

Land, check in, lie down. Drink water. Two litres before dinner, three over the course of the day. Take Diamox if you've discussed it with your doctor in advance (125mg twice daily is a standard preventive dose). Eat a light dinner. Sleep early.

In the late afternoon, if your head is clear, walk slowly to **Leh Palace** — the 1630 nine-storey citadel above the old town, modelled on the Potala Palace in Lhasa. The views from the upper floors over the Indus Valley justify the gentle climb.

At sunset, drive or ride to **Shanti Stupa** on the western hill. The white dome in the last light, the town below it, the peaks burning orange behind — this is Ladakh's welcome. Accept it.

Dinner in the bazaar: thukpa or a simple Kashmiri rice dish. Bed by 9pm.

**Where to stay:** Grand Dragon Leh (5-star, ₹12,000–18,000), Stok Palace Heritage Hotel (heritage rooms ₹7,000–9,000), or Changspa guesthouses for mid-range (₹2,500–4,500).

## Day 2: Leh Valley Sightseeing — Stay Low, Collect Permits

**Altitude:** 3,524m–3,700m | **Distance:** ~60km | **Activity level:** Easy

Rest and acclimatise. Today stays low and stays rich.

Begin at the **Hall of Fame Museum** (₹200) — the Indian Army's tribute to the conflicts fought across Ladakh's borders. The exhibits are detailed and the personal stories are genuinely moving. One hour minimum.

Drive 8km to **Spituk Gompa**, a Gelugpa monastery above the Indus, where the resident monks live a life largely unchanged from the 15th century. The upper shrine is crowded with butter lamps and centuries-old statuary.

Continue to **Magnetic Hill** (30km north of Leh) for the famous optical illusion — vehicles appearing to roll uphill on a level road. Three kilometres further: **Sangam**, where the grey-green Zanskar meets the brown Indus in a clear two-tone confluence. The colours run parallel for a hundred metres before finally blending.

Afternoon: **DC Office Leh** to collect Inner Line Permits for Nubra Valley and Pangong Lake. You will need passport copies, two photos, accommodation details, and a form. Fee: ₹400 + ₹100 environmental levy + ₹150/day per protected area. Alternatively, use the LAHDC online portal the evening before. Carry printed copies — checkpoints are multiple.

Dinner and early night.

## Day 3: Leh → Khardung La (5,600m) → Nubra Valley

**Altitude:** 5,600m at pass, 3,048m in Nubra | **Distance:** 160km | **Drive time:** 4–5 hours

Leave Leh at 7am.

The road north climbs steadily for 39km to **Khardung La Pass** at 5,600 metres — one of the world's highest motorable roads, connecting Leh to the Nubra and Shyok valleys. The pass itself is windswept, cold, and thin-aired regardless of the season. Get out, photograph the sign, spend five minutes at most. The altitude can be dangerous for extended stays at this elevation.

Then the descent begins, and it is one of the great drives in Asia.

In 40 kilometres, you lose 2,500 metres of altitude. Bare rock gives way to scrub, then to the green corridor of the Nubra River. The Karakoram Range — some of the highest and most severe peaks on earth — lines the northern horizon. You enter a valley so wide and so unexpected that it takes a moment to accept that you're still in the same place.

By early afternoon, you arrive at **Hunder** and the **Silver Sand Dunes**: actual white sand dunes in a high-altitude desert, with **Bactrian camels** — double-humped, shaggy, magnificently improbable — wandering among them. A 20-minute camel ride costs ₹300.

The afternoon light here is extraordinary. Stay until 5pm.

**Where to stay:** Hunder camps and guesthouses: ₹3,500–5,500 including meals.

## Day 4: Turtuk Village — The Edge of India

**Altitude:** ~2,900m | **Distance:** 80km one way, 3 hours each way | **Activity level:** Moderate drive

Most visitors to Nubra Valley consider Hunder their final point and turn back. Turtuk lies 80km further northwest, and it is worth every kilometre.

Until 1971, Turtuk was part of Pakistan. It was captured during the Indo-Pakistani war and opened to tourists only in 2009. The village sits in a narrow river canyon near the Line of Control — the road beyond it ends, and a very different kind of geography begins.

The community is Balti Muslim — ethnically Tibetan in origin, culturally Persian and Kashmiri by centuries of trade and conquest. The architecture reflects this hybrid history: stone houses with carved wooden balconies, apricot orchards running along every available strip of irrigated land, narrow lanes that seem almost Mediterranean in their intimacy.

Walk without a fixed itinerary. Buy dried apricots from the local shops (the Turtuk variety is considered among the finest in Asia). Visit the small heritage museum in the old part of the village — it documents the region's pre-1971 history with photographs, maps, and personal objects. Talk to the shopkeepers; many speak English and are accustomed to, and genuinely interested in, travellers from far away.

Leave by 1:30pm. Return to Hunder or Diskit before dark.

Evening: **Diskit Monastery** is 3km from Hunder on a hilltop above the valley. Founded in 1963, it is the oldest and largest monastery in Nubra. The new **Maitreya Buddha statue** visible from the valley below is enormous — 32 metres tall, facing toward Pakistan. At dusk, with monks chanting inside the gompa and the valley going purple below, Diskit offers the kind of scene that Ladakh delivers almost daily.

**Where to stay:** Diskit or Hunder for the night. Similar options to Day 3.

## Day 5: Nubra → Pangong Lake via Shyok Valley

**Altitude:** 4,320m at Pangong | **Distance:** ~162km | **Drive time:** 5 hours

The road from Nubra to Pangong via the Shyok Valley is the more beautiful route to the lake — less travelled than the Leh–Chang La approach, more dramatic in its river scenery.

Leave Hunder at 8am. The road follows the Shyok River through a corridor of eroded cliffs, braided riverbed, and empty plateau. You pass small villages, occasional yak herds, and the kind of landscape that makes you understand why early Silk Road traders wrote about this region with such intensity.

Somewhere in the early afternoon, you cross the final ridge and the descent to Pangong begins.

**Pangong Tso** at 4,320 metres. 134 kilometres long. 4 kilometres wide at the tourist end, the rest stretched out toward Tibet. The colour of the water changes through the day — turquoise at noon, cobalt at 3pm, silver as the sun drops.

Check into your tented camp at **Spangmik** and walk to the water. Try not to look at it through a phone screen at first. Just stand there.

**Where to stay:** Spangmik tented camps: ₹2,500–6,000 per person including meals. Book months ahead in peak season (July–August). The better-managed camps — Pangong Retreat, Glacier Camp, Pangong Sarai — fill earliest.

## Day 6: Pangong Sunrise → Chang La → Leh

**Altitude:** Pangong 4,320m → Chang La 5,425m → Leh 3,524m | **Distance:** ~200km | **Drive time:** 5–6 hours

Up at 5am. Cold. Walk to the water.

The sunrise at Pangong is the reason people endure the altitude and the long drive. The lake is completely silent. The mountains on the far shore, still dark, begin to catch light from the top down. The water picks up the colour before anything else. Pink, then gold, then the full sharp blue of a Himalayan morning.

You have had this to yourself. By 8am, it belongs to everyone. Leave before then.

The return to Leh goes via **Chang La Pass** (5,425m) — third highest motorable road in the world. The Changthang plateau between the lake and the pass is high-altitude grazing land: vast, almost treeless, home to Changpa nomads who raise the pashmina goats whose fibre ends up in the shawls in Leh's bazaars. Watch for Kiang (wild Tibetan ass) and the occasional Himalayan fox crossing the road.

Stop at the top of Chang La. Breathe. Look south.

Descend toward Leh. Stop at **Thiksey Monastery** — 19km east of Leh, 12 storeys, the second largest monastery in Ladakh. If your timing works out (6am morning puja), the candlelit hall full of chanting monks is the finest interior scene in Ladakh. If you arrive mid-morning, the monastery is still extraordinary: its multi-storey complex of temples, assembly halls, and monk quarters built into the ridge like a small city.

Five kilometres back along the highway: **Shey Palace** (17th century, largest copper-gold Buddha in the region, quick 20-minute visit).

Back in Leh by early afternoon. Rest. Your body has earned it.

## Day 7: Alchi and Lamayuru — The Western Valley

**Altitude:** 3,100m (Alchi), 3,510m (Lamayuru) | **Distance:** ~250km round trip | **Drive time:** 6–7 hours

The Srinagar–Leh highway heading west from Leh is one of the most historically dense roads in the Himalayas. Today you follow it to two of Ladakh's most significant and undervisited monasteries.

Leave Leh at 7am. Drive 70km west along the Indus Valley to **Alchi Monastery**.

Alchi is unlike any other monastery in Ladakh. Founded in the 11th century by Rinchen Zangpo — the great translator who helped reintroduce Buddhism to western Tibet — it preserves frescoes and wooden statuary from an Indo-Kashmiri artistic tradition that no longer exists anywhere else. The paintings inside the assembly hall and the Sumstek temple combine Kashmiri, Tibetan, and Central Asian visual styles: bodhisattvas with elaborate jewellery, mandalas of extraordinary detail, faces that look almost Byzantine in their frontality. These are among the oldest and most significant Buddhist artworks in the Himalayas.

Alchi is not dramatic from the outside. It sits quietly in a grove of apricot trees in the village. That restraint is part of its power. Spend 90 minutes and hire a local guide (₹200–400) who can explain the iconography.

Continue 55km further west to **Lamayuru** — the monastery sitting in what locals call the "moonland."

The description is accurate. The landscape around Lamayuru is like nothing else in Ladakh: pale grey-yellow eroded hills, rippled and folded by ancient lake sediment deposits, surrounding a ancient monastery perched on the only solid ridge in sight. The terrain looks geologically raw, as if the rock is still deciding what shape to become.

Lamayuru Monastery dates to the 10th or 11th century — one of the oldest in Ladakh, and one of the most atmospheric. The complex of temples climbs the ridge in tiers. The main assembly hall is dark and thick with incense, centuries of butter-lamp soot blackening the walls behind the gilded statues. Monks live here year-round.

Walk the outer path around the monastery. Look at the moonland from the upper terrace. This landscape takes a moment to stop trying to compare to somewhere else, because there is nowhere else it resembles.

Return to Leh by 7pm. It is a long day of driving, but a day with two of Ladakh's finest sites that most travellers never see.

## Day 8: Leh → Tso Moriri Lake via Chumathang

**Altitude:** 4,522m at Tso Moriri | **Distance:** 220km | **Drive time:** 6 hours

Pangong Lake is famous. Tso Moriri is not. This is its greatest advantage.

Leave Leh at 7am on the Manali highway, heading south through the Indus Valley. At Upshi, turn east into the Changthang plateau. The road climbs gradually through increasingly open high-altitude grassland, passing the **Chumathang hot springs** — sulphurous thermal pools at the side of a river, used by nomads as natural bathing places. Stop for 20 minutes.

The final hour of driving climbs onto the plateau at 4,500+ metres. The land here is enormous and empty. Chang Tang means "Northern Plain" — it is one of the highest inhabited plateaus on earth, home to Changpa nomads and their yaks, horses, and the pashmina goats whose fibre drives one of Ladakh's most important trades.

**Tso Moriri** appears in the distance as a blue-white shimmer, then resolves into a full lake: 28km long, up to 7km wide, ringed by snow peaks. At 4,522 metres, it sits higher than Pangong. The water is undisturbed. The shoreline has no camps visible from a distance. There are no boats.

In May and June, **bar-headed geese and Himalayan flamingos** feed in the shallows near the marshes at the southern end. The flamingos — improbably pink, improbably tall, standing in the silver water at altitude — are one of the more surreal wildlife sightings in India.

Check into **Korzok village** at the lake's northern end. Accommodation here is simple: homestays (₹1,200–2,000 per person including meals) and basic guesthouses. The food is good — Changpa families cook dal, rice, and local bread with considerable skill. The night sky, with no light pollution, is absolute.

**Where to stay:** Korzok homestays or Tso Moriri Eco Camp. Book ahead — options are limited and fill in peak season.

## Day 9: Tso Moriri Sunrise → Return to Leh

**Altitude:** 4,522m → 3,524m | **Distance:** 220km | **Drive time:** 6 hours

The sunrise at Tso Moriri is quieter than Pangong's. There is no camp-to-camp noise, no competing alarm clocks. Just the sound of birds calling on the water and the light arriving slowly over the eastern peaks.

Walk to the lake at 5:30am. The flamingos, if it is early summer, are already moving through the shallows. The peaks on the far shore catch the first light. The reflection in the still water is so precise it looks like a mirror.

Leave Korzok at 8am. The return route via Chumathang takes 6 hours. The plateau drive is long but meditative — golden brown grassland, distant peaks, the occasional nomad camp — and you arrive in Leh in the mid-afternoon with time to rest.

Afternoon: walk Leh's old town quietly. The lanes behind the palace, the spinning prayer wheels, the smell of incense and juniper smoke. After ten days of remote plateau and high-altitude passes, the town feels warm and human.

Dinner at the best restaurant you haven't tried yet. Momos at Tibetan Kitchen. Cheese pizza at La Piazzetta. Whatever you've been saving.

## Day 10: Namgyal Tsemo, Final Market, Airport

**Altitude:** 3,524m | **Activity level:** Easy | **Last morning**

If your flight is afternoon (most are), the last morning in Leh is unhurried.

Walk up to **Namgyal Tsemo Gompa** — the small monastery directly above Leh Palace, at the very top of the ridge. It is a steep 20-minute climb through the old town, past whitewashed houses, crumbling walls, and wild roses. The monastery at the top is small and often unlocked; the views from up here, over the full length of the Indus Valley, are better even than from the palace below.

Sit for a while. Look at where you've been.

Come down and spend an hour in the **Tibetan Handicraft Cooperative** near the main bazaar — government-certified, fixed prices, quality Pashmina, woollen items, thangka paintings, and hand-spun clothing. No bargaining theatre, no pressure. Reliable quality.

Lunch at your hotel or in the bazaar. Pack the things that didn't fit properly the first time.

Transfer to Leh Airport. The drive takes ten minutes. Allow extra time in summer — security queues are slow and the terminal fills quickly.

## Before You Go | Essential Planning Tips

**Acclimatisation.** Ten days gives your body time to adjust gradually. Day 1 is still rest. The rule: no passes above 5,000m until Day 3 at minimum. Tso Moriri (Day 8) at 4,522m is safe if you've been in Leh for a week. If you feel symptoms on any day — persistent headache, confusion, loss of coordination — descend and seek medical attention.

**Permits.** Inner Line Permits are required for Nubra Valley (Days 3–5), Pangong Lake (Days 5–6), and the Changthang area including Tso Moriri (Days 8–9). Collect at the DC Office, Leh, on Day 2, or apply online via the LAHDC portal in advance. Carry photocopies of all permits — checkpoints are frequent. Cost per area: ₹400 + ₹100 + ₹150/day.

**Road conditions.** The Alchi–Lamayuru road (Day 7) is well-maintained and surfaced. The Chumathang–Tso Moriri route (Days 8–9) has sections of unpaved track, particularly after the hot springs. A Tempo Traveller or Innova Crysta with an experienced Ladakhi driver is the right choice for the full 10-day route. Budget ₹3,000–5,500/day for a car and driver.

**Best season.** Mid-May to September. For flamingos at Tso Moriri, aim for May–June. For the Hemis Festival and maximum road accessibility, July–August. For quieter roads and comfortable temperatures, September is ideal.

**Tso Moriri altitude warning.** At 4,522m, Tso Moriri sits higher than Pangong. Arriving there on Day 8 — after a week of gradual acclimatisation — is safe. Do not put it early in the itinerary.

**Budget.** Expect ₹50,000–70,000 per person for 10 days excluding international flights. This covers domestic air (Delhi–Leh–Delhi), a private car and driver for 9 days, mid-range accommodation, all permits, entry fees, and meals.

**Food on the plateau.** In Korzok (Day 8–9), restaurant options do not exist. Your homestay provides all meals. The cooking is good — simple, warming, enough. Bring chocolate and snacks for the long plateau drives.

## The Ladakh That Most People Don't See

The Nubra–Pangong circuit is magnificent. This itinerary includes it, and then continues past it.

Tso Moriri in the early morning, with no other travellers on the shore and flamingos in the shallows, is not an experience available on the standard route. The frescoes of Alchi — made by artists from Kashmir a thousand years ago, survived wars and invasions and the slow dissolution of empires — are not on any tour bus itinerary. The road through the Changthang plateau, empty and gold and enormous, is a drive most people never take.

Ten days is enough to see all of it.
