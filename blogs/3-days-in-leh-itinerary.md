# How To Spend 3 Days in Leh | A First-Timer's Complete Itinerary

Three days is not too short for Ladakh. Three rushed days would be too short. Three focused days — planned with intention, free from the pressure of reaching Pangong or Nubra — can be one of the most complete travel experiences you've ever had.

This itinerary is for the traveller who looked at the map, saw the distances, and thought: *maybe I need more time before I come*. You don't. What you need is a different plan.

The Leh Valley holds more history, more spirituality, and more visual drama per square kilometre than most destinations on earth. The monasteries alone — centuries-old, still active, visited by working monks not just tourists — deserve two full days. The third day takes you north along the valley toward the Karakoram, to landscapes that most travellers miss entirely.

You will leave wanting to come back. But you will also leave having seen something real.

## Day 1: Arrive, Rest, and Ease In

**Altitude:** 3,524m | **Activity level:** Very easy | **Rule:** No altitude gain today

The single most important thing you can do on arrival in Leh is nothing.

This sounds obvious. Almost nobody does it. They land, feel fine, and immediately want to explore. Two hours later they have a splitting headache and spend the rest of the afternoon horizontal.

The thin air at 3,524 metres means your red blood cells are working harder than they're used to. That process takes time. Give it time.

Check into your hotel. Drink water — two litres before dinner, three by the end of the day. Avoid alcohol entirely today. Eat light. Sleep early.

In the late afternoon, if you feel genuinely comfortable (no headache, no unusual shortness of breath), walk slowly up toward **Leh Palace**.

The Palace was built in 1630 by King Sengge Namgyal, the same ruler who built Stok Palace nearby. Nine storeys of rammed earth, timber, and stone, rising directly from the ridge above the old city. It is modelled loosely on the Potala Palace in Lhasa. From the upper levels, the entire Indus Valley spreads out below you: brown mountains, a silver river, patches of green irrigated farmland in the valley floor.

Take it slowly. Sit when you want to sit. You're not rushing anywhere.

At sunset, take an auto-rickshaw (₹150) to **Shanti Stupa** on the hill to the west. The white dome catches the last light and glows. Below it, Leh town is already in the shadow of the Stokh Kangri range. On the far side of the valley, snow-capped peaks are burning orange.

Sit here for 30 minutes. This is Ladakh at its clearest.

Dinner in **Leh Main Bazaar**: the restaurants along Fort Road and Changspa Lane are well-stocked with both Tibetan and Indian options. Thukpa — a broth-based noodle soup with vegetables and sometimes mutton — is the right choice after a day of travel. Light, warm, restorative.

Back to the hotel by 9pm. Sleep is the best acclimatisation tool there is.

**Where to stay:** The Grand Dragon Leh is the city's best 5-star option (₹12,000–18,000/night). For mid-range, Ladakh Residency and many Changspa guesthouses offer clean, warm rooms at ₹2,500–4,500. For character, look for homestays in Old Town: ₹800–1,500/night, usually including breakfast.

## Day 2: The Monastery Trail — East of Leh

**Altitude:** 3,500m–3,900m | **Distance driven:** ~100km | **Activity level:** Moderate | **Start time:** 5:30am

Set your alarm for 5:30am. This is not negotiable.

**Thiksey Monastery** is 19km east of Leh, and morning puja begins at 6am. If you arrive on time — the drive takes 25 minutes on an empty road — you will walk into the main assembly hall and find thirty to forty monks already seated in rows on the wooden floor, chanting sutras in low, resonant voices. Butter lamps flicker in the dark. Incense rises. A young novice monk moves along the rows pouring cups of salty butter tea (Po Cha) — visitors are offered some too. Drink it. The flavour is unlike anything you have tasted before: rich, savoury, warming.

Thiksey is 12 storeys tall and the second largest monastery in Ladakh. Most visitors come mid-morning and see only the quiet aftermath of the ceremony. Coming at 6am, you see the reason the monastery exists.

After puja, walk the upper floors. The main temple holds a 15-metre Maitreya Buddha statue, painted and gilded, its face serene in the thin mountain light coming through the narrow windows. Breakfast in the monastery canteen costs under ₹150 and comes with views across the valley.

From Thiksey, drive 4km to **Shey Palace** — the former summer residence of Ladakh's kings, dating to the 17th century. The main shrine houses the largest copper-and-gold Buddha in the region. The palace complex is quiet and unhurried at this hour; you may have it largely to yourself.

Continue 26km further to **Hemis Monastery**, 45km from Leh — the largest and wealthiest gompa in Ladakh. Founded in the 17th century, Hemis is home to over 300 monks and holds Ladakh's most famous festival: the Hemis Festival in June or July, when masked Cham dances are performed in the courtyard to thousands of spectators. Outside festival season, the monastery is serene, and the museum upstairs holds remarkable thangka paintings, ancient weapons, and silver relics. Entry ₹50.

Spend 45 minutes at Hemis, then drive back toward Leh and stop at **Rancho School** (officially: Druk Padma Karpo School) at Shey, 45km from Leh. Fans of the Bollywood film *3 Idiots* will recognise it immediately — the school featured prominently in the movie's closing scenes. But beyond the film connection, it is a genuinely remarkable institution: progressive, solar-powered, built into the landscape, and visibly thriving. The school welcomes respectful visitors.

End the day at **Matho Monastery**, 26km from Leh — a compact 16th-century gompa belonging to the Sakya school, the only monastery of this lineage in Ladakh. Smaller and less visited than Hemis or Thiksey, Matho rewards those who come. The oracle festival held here each spring is one of Ladakh's stranger and more compelling events.

Back in Leh by 5pm. Walk the evening market. Dinner at La Piazzetta (wood-fired pizza at altitude — better than it has any right to be) or Tibetan Kitchen on Fort Road.

**Note on altitudes:** All of today's driving stays in the Indus Valley, between 3,400m and 3,900m. This is well within safe range for Day 2, provided you rested properly yesterday.

## Day 3: North of Leh — Valley Views, History & The Edge of the High Pass

**Altitude:** 3,500m–3,800m (optional viewpoint stop at 3,800m) | **Distance driven:** ~50km | **Activity level:** Easy–Moderate

Your body is now acclimatised enough to drive a short distance north — toward the Karakoram and the road that leads eventually to Khardung La Pass.

Today you will not go to Khardung La itself. That pass sits at 5,600m and requires proper acclimatisation — ideally three or more nights in Leh. What you will do is drive far enough to see the valley open into something grand.

Start at the **Hall of Fame Museum** on the northern edge of Leh (₹200 entry). Built by the Indian Army, the museum documents the conflicts fought in Ladakh and the extraordinary human stories behind them: the 1962 China war, the 1971 Pakistan war, the 1999 Kargil conflict. The exhibits include weapons, uniforms, photographs, and personal letters. It is sobering, well-curated, and worth an hour.

Drive 8km south-west to **Spituk Gompa**, a yellow-hat Gelugpa monastery perched on a rocky ridge above the confluence of the Indus River and the main highway. The monastery dates to the 11th century. Walk up through the outer courtyards to the main temple. The climb is short but steep — five minutes of careful uphill walking. At the top, the panoramic view of the Indus plain and the surrounding ranges is extraordinary.

Continue 22km north along the Srinagar–Leh highway to **Magnetic Hill**. The hill is an optical illusion — the surrounding terrain creates a false horizon that makes a flat section of road appear to slope uphill. Put your car in neutral here and it appears to roll upward. It does not. But it looks exactly like it does. The local belief is that the hill has magnetic properties powerful enough to pull vehicles; the physics is more prosaic, but the effect is convincing and good-humoured.

Three kilometres on: the **Sangam viewpoint**, where the grey-green **Zanskar River** meets the muddy brown **Indus**. Two rivers, two colours, two different mountain systems meeting in one place. The colours don't immediately blend — you can see a clear line where they meet for several hundred metres downstream. Rafting trips on the Zanskar River originate near here (₹1,500–2,500 for a short stretch; available May–June before water levels rise).

For those who want one final look north: the road to Khardung La continues beyond Leh. The **South Pullu Check Post** sits at approximately 3,800m, just 18km from the city. The views from the road below South Pullu — looking back across Leh town, across the full width of the Indus Valley, with the Stokh Kangri range (6,121m) visible to the south — are exceptional. This is as far as you should go today, and it is more than enough.

Return to Leh for lunch. Afternoon: **handicraft shopping**. The main shopping streets for quality goods are Fort Road (Pashmina, thangka paintings, Ladakhi silver) and the old town lanes around Leh Palace (local craftspeople, prayer flags, traditional wooden items). Be patient, compare prices, and don't buy from the first shop.

If your flight is early tomorrow, pack tonight. If you have an afternoon flight, you have time for a final hour sitting in the old town, watching Leh go about its day — monks walking, traders calling, children in school uniforms, prayer wheels spinning in the wind.

Leh is a living town, not a museum. Three days is enough time to start feeling that.

## Before You Go | Essential Planning Tips

**Acclimatisation is everything.** Three days in Leh means your first day must be genuine rest. Skip the temptation to explore for "just a few hours" after arrival. Give your body the afternoon. Everything on Day 2 will be better for it.

**The altitude ceiling for this itinerary.** Staying below 4,000m for a 3-day visit is the safe approach. The South Pullu viewpoint at 3,800m is fine on Day 3 if you have had no headaches. Do not attempt Khardung La (5,600m) on a 3-day trip — the altitude gain is too abrupt.

**Morning puja at Thiksey.** This is the single best thing you can do in the Leh Valley. The ceremony runs every day, beginning at 6am. Arrive before 6am, sit quietly at the back, do not use flash photography, and follow the lead of any monastery staff. It is free. Donations are welcome.

**Best season.** Mid-May to September. June and September are the sweet spots: fewer crowds, all roads and monasteries open, pleasant daytime temperatures (18–24°C), cold nights (5–10°C). July and August are peak season — busier and slightly warmer.

**Getting there.** Leh Airport (IXL) is served by IndiGo, Air India, and SpiceJet from Delhi (1.5 hours). Book well in advance — seats go fast in summer. Morning flights are less prone to weather delays.

**Packing for 3 days.** A warm fleece and a down jacket for evenings. SPF 50+ sunscreen — UV radiation at altitude is intense even on cloudy days. Comfortable walking shoes (you don't need hiking boots for this itinerary). A reusable water bottle. Lip balm. Portable charger.

**Budget.** Three days in Leh, including mid-range accommodation, a car and driver for Day 2 and Day 3, all entry fees, and meals: approximately ₹12,000–18,000 per person. A good local car with driver for a full day runs ₹2,500–3,500.

## Three Days Is Enough to Fall in Love With Ladakh

The traveller who worries that three days isn't enough usually comes back for seven.

This itinerary does not try to do everything. It does the right things. Thiksey at dawn. The dual rivers at Sangam. The palace at golden hour. A monastery trail that would exhaust a week in any other country.

Three days in Leh is a real trip. Come with an open schedule, a rested body, and the willingness to sit still on the banks of the Indus — and you will leave with more than you expected.
