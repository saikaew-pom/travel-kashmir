# The Perfect 7-Day Ladakh Itinerary | Leh, Nubra, Pangong & Back

You land at Leh Airport at 3,524 metres and everything feels slightly unreal. The air is thin. The mountains are enormous. The sky is a shade of blue that has no name in English.

Seven days is exactly enough time to see the best of Ladakh without rushing it. This itinerary takes you north over Khardung La Pass — one of the world's highest motorable roads — into the sand dunes of Nubra Valley, across to the remote Balti village of Turtuk, and then east to Pangong Lake, where the water changes colour as you watch.

This is the route our guests ask for most. Here is every detail you need to follow it.

## Day 1: Arrive Leh — Rest, Acclimatise, Explore Gently

**Altitude:** 3,524m | **Activity level:** Easy | **Distance driven:** 0km

Your body needs 48 hours to adjust to Ladakh's altitude. This is not optional advice. It is physiology. Headaches, nausea, and fatigue are common on day one — the answer is water, rest, and patience.

Check into your hotel and lie down for two to three hours after arrival. Drink three litres of water throughout the day. Avoid alcohol entirely on day one.

By late afternoon, if you feel steady, walk slowly up to **Leh Palace**. Built in 1630, it is nine storeys of rammed earth and timber rising above the old city. The views from the top floor are worth the gentle climb. Take your time. Sit when you need to.

At sunset, hire a local auto-rickshaw (₹150) to **Shanti Stupa** on the hill west of the city. The stupa glows white against a purple sky. Pangong-bound travellers always wish they had come here sooner.

Dinner in **Leh Main Bazaar**: try thukpa (Tibetan noodle soup) or a basic dal-rice at any of the Kashmiri dhabas along Fort Road. Eat light. Sleep by 9pm.

**Where to stay:** Stok Palace Heritage Hotel (heritage rooms from ₹7,000), or any mid-range guesthouse in the Old Town area (₹2,000–3,500/night).

## Day 2: Leh Sightseeing — Lower Altitude, Permit Collection

**Altitude:** 3,524m–3,700m | **Activity level:** Easy–Moderate | **Distance driven:** ~60km

Today you stay low and let your body continue adjusting. The sights are genuinely excellent — and you need to collect your Inner Line Permits before Day 3.

Start at the **Hall of Fame Museum** (₹200), a detailed tribute to the Indian Army's role in Ladakh. Sobering and well-curated. Allow an hour.

Drive 8km to **Spituk Gompa**, perched on a rocky hill above the Indus River. The main assembly hall is active with monks. The views across the valley are wide and clean.

Continue 30km along the Srinagar–Leh highway to **Magnetic Hill** — where optical illusion makes vehicles appear to roll uphill on their own. It's a quirk of geometry, not gravity, but entertaining nonetheless.

From there, 3km to the **Sangam viewpoint**: the point where the grey-green Zanskar River meets the brown Indus. Two rivers, two colours, one confluence. Stand here for ten minutes.

On the way back, stop at the **DC Office in Leh** to collect your **Inner Line Permits** for Nubra Valley and Pangong Lake. The permit costs ₹400 + ₹100 environmental fee + ₹150/day per protected area. You'll need a passport copy, two photos, and your accommodation details. The process takes 30–60 minutes. You can also use the LAHDC online portal the evening before if you prefer.

Evening: walk the **Tibetan Market** on Fort Road. Pashmina shawls, thangka paintings, singing bowls, and Ladakhi jewellery. Dinner at Gesmo Restaurant — the apple crumble is famous among travellers.

## Day 3: Leh → Khardung La → Nubra Valley

**Altitude:** Up to 5,600m at Khardung La, down to 3,048m in Nubra | **Distance:** 160km | **Drive time:** 4–5 hours

This is the day Ladakh shows you what it's capable of.

Leave Leh by 7am. The first hour winds through increasingly bare mountain terrain as you climb toward **Khardung La Pass** at 5,600 metres. The road narrows. Prayer flags appear in clusters. The air becomes genuinely difficult — most people feel light-headed at the top. Get out, take a photo, spend five minutes maximum. The cold is severe and the altitude is no joke. Then descend.

The drop into Nubra Valley is one of the great drives in Asia. In 40 kilometres you lose 2,500 metres of altitude. The landscape transforms from barren rock into a wide green river valley flanked by the Karakoram Range on one side and the Ladakh Range on the other.

By early afternoon, you arrive in **Hunder** — home to the **Silver Sand Dunes**. These are not metaphorical. White sand dunes sit in a high-altitude desert between two mountain ranges, and **Bactrian camels** — double-humped, shaggy, wonderfully strange — graze among them. A 20-minute camel ride costs ₹300 per person.

The light here in late afternoon is extraordinary: pale dunes, dark mountains, thin blue sky. Stay until 5pm.

**Where to stay:** Hunder has a good selection of camps and guesthouses. The Himalayan Country House Camp offers comfortable tents with attached bathrooms at ₹4,000–5,500 per night including dinner and breakfast. Book ahead in July–August.

## Day 4: Nubra → Turtuk Village & Back

**Altitude:** ~2,900m | **Distance:** 80km each way (3 hours one way) | **Drive time:** 6 hours total

Most visitors to Nubra turn back at Hunder or Diskit. That is their loss.

**Turtuk** sits 80km further northwest, so close to the Pakistan border that it was part of Pakistan until the 1971 war. It was opened to tourists only in 2009. The village feels genuinely remote — not performatively remote, but actually off the circuit.

The Balti community here is Muslim. The architecture is different from Buddhist Ladakh: stone houses with wooden balconies, apricot and apple orchards running along narrow lanes, women in coloured headscarves. The pace is different too.

Walk the village without a fixed agenda. Talk to the local shopkeepers who speak good English. Visit the small local museum documenting the area's pre-1971 history. Buy a jar of apricot jam — the apricots grown here are among the best in Asia.

Leave Turtuk by 1:30pm to return to Hunder or Diskit before dark. The road is good but mountain driving after sunset is not recommended.

Evening: **Diskit Monastery** is only a few kilometres from Hunder. Founded in 1963, the monastery sits on a hilltop above the valley and looks down on a massive new Maitreya Buddha statue below. At dusk, when the monks are chanting inside and the mountains are going pink, it is the kind of scene that makes you understand why people keep coming back to Ladakh.

**Where to stay:** Return to your Hunder camp for the night, or move to Diskit (similar options, ₹3,500–5,000).

## Day 5: Nubra → Pangong Lake via Shyok Valley

**Altitude:** 4,350m at Pangong | **Distance:** ~162km | **Drive time:** 5 hours

The road from Nubra to Pangong via the **Shyok Valley** is relatively new — less travelled than the Leh route and more spectacular. It follows the Shyok River through a corridor of eroded cliff faces, braided riverbeds, and occasional nomad encampments.

Leave Hunder by 8am. The drive is long but never dull. Sometime around midday you cross the last mountain shoulder and the road begins to descend toward Pangong.

The first glimpse of **Pangong Tso** is a physical event. You come around a corner and the lake is simply there: 134 kilometres long, 4 kilometres wide at this point, and a colour that is simultaneously turquoise and sapphire and something in between. Seventy-five percent of the lake lies in Tibet.

Check into your tented camp at **Spangmik** (the main tourist village on the western shore) and then walk to the water. Don't rush to photograph it. Just look at it for a while.

The colour of the lake changes through the day — brightest turquoise in morning, deepening to cobalt blue in the afternoon, silver-grey as the sun drops behind the mountains. Try to be outside for all three.

**Where to stay:** Spangmik tented camps: ₹2,500–6,000 per person including meals. The mid-range camps are fine — the lake is the same from all of them. Book ahead; the good camps fill up weeks in advance in peak season (July–August).

## Day 6: Pangong Sunrise → Chang La → Leh

**Altitude:** Pangong 4,320m → Chang La 5,425m → Leh 3,524m | **Distance:** ~200km | **Drive time:** 5–6 hours

Set an alarm for 5am.

The sunrise at Pangong is not like other sunrises. The lake has no trees, no buildings, no noise. Just water, mountains, and light arriving in stages: first a pale lemon line on the horizon, then pink on the peaks, then the full gold of an unobstructed Himalayan morning hitting the surface of the water.

You will stand there in the cold and understand exactly why people fly to Leh just for this.

Leave the lake by 8am. The return to Leh goes via **Chang La Pass** (5,425m) — the third highest motorable road in the world. Stop at the top, breathe the thin air, and take the obligatory photograph at the sign.

Descend through the Changthang plateau. Watch for herds of pashmina goats and the occasional Himalayan fox on the roadside.

Around midday, stop at **Thiksey Monastery** — 19km east of Leh, 12 storeys tall, unmistakable on its hilltop. If you left early enough, you may catch the morning puja (prayers begin at 6am; the monks chanting in the candlelit hall, a young monk assigned to pour butter tea for visitors). Otherwise, the monastery is still magnificent. The views of the Indus Valley from the upper floors are among the finest in all of Ladakh.

Five kilometres further: **Shey Palace**, former summer residence of Ladakhi royalty, housing the largest copper-gold Buddha in the region. Entry is quick — 20 minutes is enough.

Arrive back in Leh by 2–3pm. Rest. Walk the bazaar. Buy the things you meant to buy on Day 2.

**Where to stay:** Return to your Leh hotel.

## Day 7: Morning Free → Airport Transfer

**Altitude:** 3,524m | **Activity level:** Easy

Morning flights from Leh leave as early as 6am — confirm your departure time and plan accordingly.

If you have the morning, Leh rewards a slow final walk. The **Tibetan Market** on Fort Road is best before 10am, when shopkeepers are still unhurried and willing to talk. Look for genuine Ladakhi silver jewellery, hand-woven woollen items, and apricot products from Nubra.

The **Old Town lanes** behind Leh Palace — narrow alleys lined with whitewashed houses, prayer flags overhead, the smell of juniper smoke from temple offerings — are best explored without a phone in your hand.

Most flights depart in the morning. The drive to Leh Airport takes 10 minutes from the city. Leave more time than you think you need — check-in queues in peak season move slowly.

## Before You Go | Essential Planning Tips

**Permits.** Nubra Valley and Pangong Lake both require **Inner Line Permits**. Collect them at the DC Office in Leh on Day 2, or apply online via the LAHDC portal at least 24 hours before your trip. Cost: ₹400 + ₹100 environment fee + ₹150/day. Carry photocopies — you will be checked at multiple checkpoints.

**Acclimatisation.** Do not skip Day 1 rest. Do not visit Khardung La before Day 3. Altitude sickness can be serious. Symptoms: persistent headache, dizziness, shortness of breath at rest, or loss of coordination. If severe symptoms develop, descend immediately. Diamox (acetazolamide, 125mg twice daily) can help — consult your doctor before the trip.

**Best season.** Mid-May through September. Monsoon rains affect Leh less than other Himalayan destinations — the rain shadow effect keeps it relatively dry. July–August is peak season: all roads open, maximum crowds. June and September offer better weather balance and fewer tourists.

**Packing essentials.** Sunscreen SPF 50+ (UV intensity at altitude is severe), warm layers (nights drop to 5°C even in July), good walking shoes, a rain jacket, a portable charger (long drives), lip balm, and altitude medication.

**Flights.** Book Leh flights (IndiGo, Air India, GoAir from Delhi) at least 6–8 weeks ahead in summer. Flights fill fast and prices spike. Morning flights have fewer weather cancellations.

**Budget.** Expect ₹35,000–55,000 per person for 7 days excluding international flights — covering domestic flights, accommodation (mid-range), a private car with driver, permits, entry fees, and meals.

**Food.** Do not leave Ladakh without eating: Thukpa (hearty Tibetan noodle soup), Momos (steamed dumplings, best with a chilli sauce), Skyu (pasta stew slow-cooked with root vegetables), and — if you're adventurous — Po Cha, the famous salty butter tea made with yak butter. Acquired taste. Very much worth acquiring.
