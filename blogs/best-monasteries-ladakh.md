# The Best Monasteries in Ladakh | Thiksey, Hemis, Diskit & More

Ladakh has more than 100 monasteries. They sit on clifftops, nestle into gorge walls, crown hilltops above ancient towns, and hide in valleys that took three days to reach before the roads came.

Most travellers visit two or three. The ones who slow down and choose carefully come away with something that stays with them: the sound of a drum echoing through a candlelit hall at 6am, a monk's robe catching the wind on a rooftop, murals painted in the 11th century still vivid on cold stone walls.

Ladakh is known for its landscapes. But its monasteries — called gompas in Tibetan — are the heart of the place. This guide helps you choose which ones to visit, and tells you exactly what to look for when you get there.

## The Essential Monasteries

### Thiksey Monastery — Come at 6am

**Location:** 19km east of Leh | **Altitude:** 3,600m | **Founded:** 15th century | **Entry:** ₹50

Thiksey is the monastery most people picture when they imagine Ladakh. Twelve storeys of white-and-ochre buildings climb a rocky hillside in a form modelled on the Potala Palace in Lhasa. From the road below, it's monumental. From the roof, the Indus Valley spreads in every direction.

But the right way to visit Thiksey is at 6am, for morning puja. Monks gather in the main assembly hall — butter lamps flickering, drums and cymbals building into a resonant wall of sound, chanting that comes from deep in the chest. The air smells of juniper incense. It lasts about an hour. Visitors are welcome to observe quietly from the side of the hall.

The monastery also holds a 15-metre Maitreya (future Buddha) statue built in 1980, one of the largest in Ladakh. The two-storey figure is adorned in gold paint and coloured jewels; the ground floor serves as the shrine room, the upper floor offers the face and crown up close.

**Best time to visit:** Year-round; morning puja daily at 6am. Most atmospheric in winter when snow dusts the roof.

### Hemis Monastery — The Largest and the Wealthiest

**Location:** 45km southeast of Leh | **Altitude:** 3,600m | **Founded:** 1672 | **Entry:** ₹100

Hemis sits in a hidden valley that feels deliberately concealed. The approach road winds between cliff walls, and then suddenly the monastery opens before you — a large courtyard bounded by red buildings, prayer flags strung between the rooftops, a courtyard large enough for the festivals it was built to hold.

It is the largest and wealthiest monastery in Ladakh, and it is the home of the Hemis Festival — called Tsechu — held each June or July on the anniversary of Guru Rinpoche's birth (the exact date follows the Tibetan lunar calendar, so check the year you're travelling). Monks in elaborate silk costumes and painted masks perform Cham, ritual dances that dramatise the victory of good over evil. The courtyard fills with pilgrims and tourists, and the energy is unlike anything else in Ladakh. If you can time your visit to coincide with it, do.

Outside festival time, Hemis rewards a slower visit: the thangka paintings in the assembly hall are among the finest in Ladakh, and the museum holds rare Buddhist texts, silver stupas, and ancient artefacts. Hemis is also the primary research base for the Snow Leopard Conservancy India Trust. In winter, this valley is one of the best places on earth to spot wild snow leopards.

**Best time to visit:** June–July for the Hemis Festival; December–February for snow leopard sightings.

### Alchi Monastery — Where the Art Is Different

**Location:** 70km west of Leh | **Altitude:** 3,100m | **Founded:** 11th century | **Entry:** ₹50

Alchi is the oldest continuously active monastery in Ladakh, and the one most unlike any other. While Ladakh's other major gompas follow the high-altitude Tibetan architectural tradition — white-painted stone, hilltop position, fortress silhouette — Alchi is built on flat ground beside the Indus River. It looks almost modest from the outside.

Inside is another matter. The murals are extraordinary. Dating from the 11th and 12th centuries, they blend Kashmiri, Central Asian, and early Tibetan artistic traditions in a style that was never replicated anywhere else. The figures are elongated and expressive, the colours still rich despite their age, the detail — embroidered cloth patterns, jewellery, facial expressions — extraordinarily intricate. Art historians consider Alchi one of the most important Buddhist art sites in the world.

Photography is not permitted inside the shrines, which enforces genuine looking. Allow at least 90 minutes. The monastery is managed by Likir Monastery and a resident monk is usually present to explain the iconography.

**Best time to visit:** May–October (road conditions permitting; can be combined with Magnetic Hill and Sangam Viewpoint on the same day-trip).

### Spituk Monastery — Ancient Ground Above Leh

**Location:** 8km south of Leh, near the airport | **Altitude:** 3,390m | **Founded:** 11th century | **Entry:** ₹30

Spituk is the monastery you pass on the road from the airport, perched on a conical hill above the Indus floodplain. It was founded in the 11th century and belongs to the Gelugpa school of Tibetan Buddhism — the same tradition as the Dalai Lama. The monastery is home to around 100 monks and is one of Leh's oldest religious sites.

The top terrace holds a shrine to the fierce protector deity Mahakali — whose face is kept veiled and unveiled only during the Gustor Festival in January, when butter-lamp processions and mask dances fill the courtyard. The views from the rooftop at sunset, looking back toward Leh with the Stok Kangri range behind, are among the best easy-access views near the town.

**Best time to visit:** Year-round; Gustor Festival in January.

### Diskit Monastery — The Soul of Nubra Valley

**Location:** 150km north of Leh, Nubra Valley | **Altitude:** 3,100m | **Founded:** 1963 | **Entry:** ₹30**

Diskit is the oldest monastery in Nubra Valley, established by Changzem Tserab Zangpo in 1963 under the patronage of the Dalai Lama. It occupies a ridge above the valley floor, with views stretching across the wide, flat expanse of the Shyok River and the dunes at Hunder to the north.

The defining feature is the giant Maitreya Buddha statue — 32 metres high, consecrated by the Dalai Lama in 2010 — that faces northwest toward Pakistan. It's visible from kilometres away. Up close, the statue is remarkable for its golden finish and benign expression; the contrast between its monumental scale and the desert emptiness around it is quietly affecting.

The monastery interior is relatively small and intimate. One hall displays a collection of demon heads said to represent expelled evil spirits — a tradition specific to Diskit. Monks here are generally approachable and the atmosphere is less touristed than the major gompas near Leh.

**Best time to visit:** May–September; combine with Hunder Sand Dunes (12km) for a half-day in Nubra.

### Shey Palace — The Copper-Gold Buddha

**Location:** 15km east of Leh | **Altitude:** 3,350m | **Founded:** 17th century | **Entry:** ₹30

Shey was the ancient summer capital of Ladakh and the seat of Ladakhi royalty before Leh took over that role. The palace complex, now partly ruined, climbs a rocky outcrop above the village. The main monastery within the complex holds Ladakh's largest copper-gilt Buddha — a seated Shakyamuni figure nearly 7.5 metres high, dressed in gold paint and precious stones, which fills the entire height of the three-storey shrine room.

It's less visited than Thiksey (visible from the same road) and often less crowded. The combination of the deteriorating palace walls, the ancient rock carvings at the base of the hill, and the overwhelming golden Buddha inside makes Shey worth the separate stop.

**Best time to visit:** April–October; morning light is best for the shrine interior.

### Phugtal Monastery — Built Into a Cliff Face

**Location:** Zanskar Valley, ~250km from Leh | **Altitude:** 3,900m | **Founded:** 12th century | **Entry:** donation**

Phugtal is one of the most dramatically located monasteries in all of Asia. It occupies a large natural cave hollowed into a near-vertical cliff above the Tsarap River, in the deepest part of Zanskar Valley. From a distance, the white and ochre buildings appear to grow directly out of the rockface, dozens of metres above the river.

It is accessible only on foot: the final stretch from the nearest road is a 7–9 kilometre trek through rugged gorge terrain. That barrier is exactly why it remains authentic. Around 70 monks live here year-round. The monastery dates to the 12th century and holds rare Buddhist texts and ancient frescoes. The cave itself is believed to have been used as a meditation retreat by the sage Gangsem Sherap Sampo.

Visiting Phugtal requires at minimum a two-day commitment and ideally forms part of the Zanskar Valley trek. It is not a casual day trip — which is precisely why it rewards those who make the effort.

**Best time to visit:** June–September (Zanskar road opens); requires trekking experience and a guide.

## Practical Notes for Visiting Monasteries in Ladakh

Entry fees at most gompas range from ₹30 to ₹100 for foreign tourists. Photography is permitted in most exterior areas; many assembly halls prohibit photography of murals and statues — look for signs and ask if uncertain. Dress modestly: covered shoulders, covered knees, shoes removed before entering shrines.

Morning is universally the best time to visit any monastery. Monks rise early, rituals happen early, light is better, and tour groups arrive later. For Thiksey, 6am is the number. For everywhere else, aim to arrive by 8–9am.

If you have time for only two, choose Thiksey for the morning puja and Hemis for depth of history and art — or Alchi if you have any interest in ancient Buddhist painting. Add Diskit if you're making the Nubra Valley overnight trip, which you should be.
