# How To Get Around Ladakh | Taxis, Shared Jeeps & Self-Drive

You land in Leh. You step outside the airport. You open Google Maps and search for an Uber. Nothing. You try Ola. Nothing.

Welcome to Ladakh. There is no ride-sharing here. No buses run to the tourist sites. The transport system works entirely differently — and once you understand it, it is actually quite good. You just need to know the rules.

Here is everything you need to know about getting around Ladakh.

## The Foundation: Leh's Union Taxi System

Ladakh operates on a Union Taxi system. Every licensed taxi belongs to a union, and every route has a fixed government-set fare. This is not a negotiation-based market. The price is what it is.

**Where the taxi stands are:**
- **Leh Main Taxi Stand** — near Main Bazaar, behind the main market. This is your primary hub for all major excursions.
- **Leh Tourism Complex** — near the bus stand. Used less frequently for tourist routes.

Walk up to the stand. Tell the dispatcher where you want to go. They will assign a vehicle and give you the fixed fare. Tipping is not standard but is appreciated for multi-day trips.

**What vehicles you'll find:** Mostly Mahindra Boleros, Toyota Innova Crystas, and Mahindra Scorpios. All suitable for mountain roads. The Bolero is the workhorse — robust, high clearance, slightly less comfortable.

**One thing to sort before you get in:** Agree on all stops, waiting times, and whether fuel is included. In Ladakh, the standard fare typically does NOT include fuel surcharges on certain remote routes. Ask directly: "Is fuel included in this fare?" Get a clear yes or no. This small conversation prevents a very awkward one at the end of the trip.

## Private Taxi vs. Shared Jeep: The Real Trade-Off

You have two options for almost every route: hire a private vehicle or join a shared jeep.

### Private Taxi
You book the whole vehicle. You control the itinerary, the stops, the pace. If you want to spend 45 minutes photographing a monastery, you can. If you want to leave at 5am to catch dawn light at Pangong, you can. The driver waits at every stop.

For groups of 3 or more, a private taxi often works out cheaper per person than flying solo — and gives you far more flexibility.

### Shared Jeep
You pay per seat. The vehicle fills up with other travellers going the same direction. It leaves when it's full — which might mean waiting an hour at the stand. You stop where the driver decides to stop, for as long as the driver decides.

Shared jeeps are fine for one-way transfers where you just need to get from A to B. They are not ideal for anything with sightseeing involved.

**The honest truth:** For Nubra, Pangong, and Tso Moriri, pay for a private taxi. The cost difference per person (when split between 2–4 people) is small. The difference in experience is large.

## Key Route Fares (Government Rates, 2024–25)

These are approximate government-set fares for private taxis. Shared jeep fares are in brackets.

| Route | Distance | Private Taxi | Shared (per person) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leh → Khardung La → Nubra Valley | 160km | ₹3,500–4,500/day | ₹500–700 |
| Leh → Pangong Lake (Chang La) | 160km | ₹3,500–4,000/day | ₹600–800 |
| Leh → Hemis Monastery | 45km | ₹1,200 | ₹150–200 |
| Leh → Tso Moriri | 240km | ₹5,000–6,000/day | ₹800–1,000 |
| Leh → Kargil | 234km | ₹5,000–6,000 | ₹600–800 |
| Leh → Alchi | 70km | ₹1,800–2,200 | ₹200–250 |
| Leh → Magnetic Hill | 30km | ₹800–1,000 | — |

**Multi-day packages are cheaper.** If you're doing Nubra for 2 nights, book the taxi for 2 days rather than hiring one-way on both ends. Drivers prefer multi-day work and the rate per day drops. A 2-day Nubra taxi is ₹6,500–8,000 rather than ₹3,500 twice.

Similarly, a common 5-day circuit — Leh → Nubra (2 nights) → Pangong (1 night) → Leh — can often be arranged with a single driver for ₹14,000–18,000 total. Agree the full itinerary in advance and put it in writing (a WhatsApp message to the driver works fine as a record).

## How to Hire a Taxi: Step by Step

**Step 1: Go to the Main Taxi Stand in person.**
Do this the evening before your excursion. Drivers who are booked in advance will be ready and waiting. Don't assume you can turn up at 6am and find a driver ready to go.

**Step 2: State your full itinerary clearly.**
Tell the dispatcher or driver exactly where you want to go, which sites you want to stop at, how long you plan to stay at each, and what time you want to leave. Be specific. "Pangong Lake with stops at Chang La, Durbuk, and Spangmik" is better than "Pangong."

**Step 3: Agree the full fare, in writing.**
Ask the dispatcher to write it on a receipt or get the driver to confirm the total via WhatsApp. Include: route, number of days, overnight waiting charge (if applicable), and whether fuel and road tolls are included.

**Step 4: Agree the departure time.**
High-altitude roads are better tackled in the morning. For Pangong and Nubra, a 6–7am departure from Leh is standard and smart.

**Step 5: Pay a deposit if the driver asks.**
50% upfront for multi-day trips is normal. Carry cash — most drivers do not use UPI.

## Hiring for Multiple Days: What to Negotiate

When booking a driver for 3+ days, a few things are worth clarifying upfront:

- **Who pays for the driver's accommodation and meals?** Standard practice is the tourist pays ₹300–500/night for the driver's guesthouse. This is fair — the driver is away from home. Most multi-day packages include this.
- **What happens if roads are blocked?** Agree in advance that if a pass closes due to landslide or weather, the driver will attempt an alternate route or wait — and that you won't be charged extra for the delay.
- **Waiting time at sites.** Most drivers are perfectly happy to wait. Some charge an additional ₹200–300 per hour for waits over 2 hours at a site. Confirm before you start.
- **Return to Leh.** If your trip ends at Pangong and you want to go back via a different route (e.g., via Tso Kar or Hanle), agree this in advance. Drivers charge more for non-standard returns.

## Self-Drive: Royal Enfield Rentals

If you're an experienced rider and want to do Ladakh on a motorcycle, Leh has a solid rental market. Royal Enfield Bullets and Himalayans dominate — well-suited for the altitude and terrain.

**Cost:** ₹1,200–1,800 per day depending on the model. Himalayan 411cc is the most popular and the most practical. Classic 350 is also available.

**What you need:**
- A valid driving licence from your home country plus an International Driving Permit (IDP) — required for all foreign nationals.
- Experience riding in mountain terrain. This is not the place to learn.
- A helmet (usually provided, but bring your own if you have one).

**Rental shops on Fort Road** in Leh are the main cluster. Inspect the bike carefully before you take it — check brakes, tyres, lights, and horn. Take photos of any existing damage. Get the rental agreement in writing.

**Fuel:** Fill up in Leh every time before heading out. Petrol pumps are rare beyond town and can run dry in peak season. Carry a small emergency fuel container if going to remote areas.

**One honest caution:** The altitude amplifies physical strain significantly. Riding a motorcycle at 5,000m+ feels very different from riding at sea level. Take the first day to acclimatise fully in Leh before getting on a bike. Fatigue and altitude are a dangerous combination.

## The Thing Everyone Forgets

There is one question that causes more end-of-trip arguments than anything else in Ladakh: fuel.

Ask your driver, before you get in the vehicle: **"Is fuel included in the fare?"**

On most Union Taxi routes, fuel is included in the fixed fare. But on some extended remote routes — particularly Tso Moriri, Hanle, and anything crossing into Zanskar — drivers will ask for a fuel supplement. This is not necessarily unreasonable, but if you haven't agreed it in advance, it creates friction.

The solution: ask the question. Write down the answer. No surprises.

## Quick Practical Notes

- **No ATMs in Nubra or at Pangong.** Withdraw cash in Leh before every excursion. SBI and PNB ATMs are reliable on Fort Road and near the Main Bazaar.
- **No mobile signal beyond Leh.** Jio and BSNL work in town. Outside town, you're offline. Download offline Google Maps for every route before you leave.
- **Petrol pumps:** There is one in Karu (on the way to Pangong), one in Diskit (Nubra), and one in Durbuk. That's roughly the full list for tourist routes. Fill up before you go.
- **Inner Line Permits:** Required for Nubra, Pangong, Tso Moriri, and Hanle. Your driver will not take you without them. Get them the morning after arrival at the DC Office in Leh (open 10am–1pm) or online at lahdclehpermit.in. Bring your passport (foreigners) or Aadhaar (Indian nationals).
