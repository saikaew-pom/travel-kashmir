# How To Get To Leh Ladakh | Flights, Roads & What To Know

You've decided you're going to Ladakh. Good decision. Now comes the first real question: how do you actually get there?

Leh sits at 3,524 metres above sea level in the Himalayas. It is not on the way to anywhere. Getting there requires a deliberate choice — by air, by road from Manali, or by road from Srinagar. Each option has its own costs, risks, and rewards. The right choice depends entirely on your time, budget, and what kind of traveller you are.

Here is an honest breakdown of all three.

## Option 1: Flying to Leh (Best for Time-Poor Travellers)

**The airport:** Kushok Bakula Rimpochee Airport (IATA: LEH) sits at 3,256m — one of the highest commercial airports in the world. The runway is short, the approaches are dramatic, and on a clear morning the views of the Himalayas during descent are genuinely extraordinary.

**The flight:** Delhi to Leh takes 1 hour 20 minutes. Direct flights also operate from Mumbai, Chandigarh, Jammu, and Srinagar. Airlines on the route include IndiGo, Air India, SpiceJet, and Vistara.

**The timing rule:** Always book a morning flight. This is not optional advice — it is practical survival. By mid-morning, thermal turbulence builds over the mountains and afternoon flights are frequently delayed or cancelled. The airport shuts operations when visibility drops. Morning flights (before 10am) land reliably. Afternoon flights do not.

**Cost:** Economy fares range from ₹3,500–8,000 Delhi–Leh if booked 2–3 months in advance. Peak summer (June–August) sells out fast. Book early or pay significantly more.

**What happens after you land:**

This is where most first-time visitors make a costly mistake. You step off the plane at 3,256m. Your body has no idea what is happening. Do not go for a walk. Do not immediately throw your bags onto a bike and head to Khardung La. The first day in Leh is a rest day, full stop.

Here is what to do:

1. Reach your hotel or guesthouse. Take a slow rickshaw — ₹150–200 from the airport to Main Bazaar.
2. Eat a light lunch. Soup and bread. No alcohol.
3. Lie down for two to three hours. Even if you feel fine.
4. Drink two to three litres of water through the day.
5. Walk slowly around the market in the late afternoon if you feel well enough.
6. Sleep early.

Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) symptoms — headache, nausea, dizziness — typically arrive 6–12 hours after ascent. Resting on day one prevents most of it.

**Verdict on flying:** Ideal if you have 7 days or fewer, are travelling with family or older parents, or simply want to get to Ladakh without two days on mountain roads. It is the fastest and most predictable option.

## Option 2: Manali–Leh Highway (Best for the Adventure Traveller)

The Manali–Leh Highway is 490km of high-altitude mountain road crossing five passes. It is one of the great road journeys in Asia. It is also genuinely demanding.

**The passes:** Rohtang La (3,978m), Baralacha La (4,890m), Lachung La (5,065m), Naki La (4,739m), Tanglang La (5,328m). You cross all five in roughly two days.

**The timing:** This road is open late May to late September. In July and August it is at its most accessible but also most crowded. Snow can close passes without warning even in summer.

**The itinerary:**

Most travellers do it in two days:

- **Day 1:** Manali → Jispa (roughly 100km, 4–5 hours). Jispa sits at 3,200m and is a good first night — higher than Manali but not so extreme that acclimatisation becomes a problem. Alternatively, push on to Sarchu (4,253m) in one day, but this is a long drive and altitude hits harder here.
- **Day 2:** Jispa or Sarchu → Leh (320–390km, 8–10 hours). This is a big day. Start early — by 6am — to avoid afternoon mud and have buffer time for pass delays.

**Cost:**
- Shared taxi, Manali to Leh: ₹1,000–1,500 per person. You share a Sumo or Innova with strangers. Packed but cheap.
- Private taxi: ₹10,000–14,000 for the whole vehicle. Much more comfortable. Worth splitting with a small group.
- Royal Enfield: Renting a motorcycle in Manali (₹1,000–1,500/day) and riding the highway yourself is a rite of passage for many travellers. Requires experience on mountain roads and ideally a co-rider.

**The acclimatisation advantage:** Because you gain altitude gradually over two days, your body adjusts far better than a flight. Travellers who drive to Leh typically feel better in the first 48 hours than those who fly. This matters if you're planning to go straight to Nubra or Pangong.

**What can go wrong:** Landslides, washed-out sections, military convoys that block the road for hours, and mechanical breakdowns in places with no phone signal. This is not fearmongering — it is the road. Build a buffer day into your schedule.

**Verdict on Manali–Leh:** Best for travellers who have 10+ days, want the experience of the drive itself as part of the journey, and are reasonably fit and comfortable with long days in a vehicle on mountain roads.

## Option 3: Srinagar–Leh Highway (Best for Gentle Acclimatisation)

The Srinagar–Leh Highway (NH1) is 434km and runs through Kargil. It is less dramatic than the Manali route, less talked about, and arguably better for your body.

**Why it works for acclimatisation:** You start in Srinagar at around 1,600m. The road climbs steadily. You sleep in Kargil at 2,676m. You arrive in Leh at 3,524m. The altitude gain is gentler than both flying and the Manali route, which makes it genuinely useful if you're sensitive to altitude.

**The passes:** The main obstacle is Zojila Pass at 3,528m — far lower than anything on the Manali route. The road is better surfaced for most of its length.

**The itinerary:**
- **Day 1:** Srinagar → Kargil (204km, 5–6 hours). Stay overnight in Kargil.
- **Day 2:** Kargil → Leh (230km, 5–6 hours). Pass through Lamayuru, one of Ladakh's oldest monasteries — worth a stop.

**Open season:** April to November. This is the longest open window of any route — useful if you're travelling in shoulder season.

**Cost:**
- Shared taxi, Srinagar to Leh: ₹800–1,000 per person.
- Private taxi: ₹8,000–12,000 for the vehicle.

**What the route adds:** Kargil is historically significant (the 1999 Kargil War was fought nearby). Drass, just before Kargil, is one of the coldest inhabited places on earth. Lamayuru Monastery looks as if it was carved directly into a lunar landscape. This is not a boring drive.

**Verdict on Srinagar–Leh:** Best if you're already travelling in Kashmir (smart combination), concerned about altitude sickness, or want a slightly lower-key road journey that leaves you feeling better on arrival.

## Booking Flights: The Practical Checklist

- Book 2–3 months ahead for summer travel (June–August). Prices spike significantly closer to the date.
- Always choose morning departures (before 10am) for Delhi–Leh. This applies to the return flight too — afternoon departures from Leh are frequently delayed.
- Check your airline's baggage policy. Some budget airlines charge for checked bags on this route.
- Download the DIGI YATRA app if you're an Indian national — it speeds up airport entry.
- Have your hotel address in Leh written down. Immigration at Leh sometimes asks for accommodation details.

## After You Arrive: The First 24 Hours in Leh

Whether you flew or drove, the first day in Leh has the same rules:

- **Rest.** Do not plan sightseeing on day one if you flew.
- **Hydrate.** Minimum 3 litres of water.
- **Eat light.** Avoid meat and alcohol for the first 24 hours.
- **Acclimatise before you ascend.** Do not head to Khardung La or Pangong until you have spent at least 2 nights in Leh (if you flew). Your safety in the high passes depends on how well you acclimatised in town first.
- **Sort your permits.** The DC Office is open 10am–1pm. Get your Inner Line Permits the morning after arrival so your excursions aren't delayed. (More on permits in our Pangong and Nubra guides.)
