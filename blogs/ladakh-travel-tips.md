# 15 Things To Know Before You Visit Ladakh | Essential Tips for First-Timers

You've booked the flight. The itinerary looks perfect — Leh, Nubra, Pangong, back. Everything is lined up. Then someone who's been to Ladakh says: "Did you sort your permits?" And a flicker of uncertainty crosses your face.

Ladakh rewards preparation more than almost any destination in Asia. Not because it's dangerous — it isn't — but because small oversights (no cash, no permit, too little rest on Day 1) compound quickly at 3,500 metres above sea level. These 15 tips are what experienced Ladakh travellers wish they'd known before their first trip.

## 1. The 48-Hour Acclimatisation Rule Is Non-Negotiable

Leh sits at 3,524 metres. Your blood oxygen drops significantly the moment you step off the plane. The Leh District Administration now enforces a **mandatory 48-hour rest period** for all travellers arriving by air — and no excursions to Khardung La (5,600m) or Chang La (5,425m) are permitted until Day 3.

This is not a suggestion. Breaking it risks Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS), which can escalate to High Altitude Pulmonary Oedema (HAPE) or High Altitude Cerebral Oedema (HACE) — both life-threatening. Even fit, healthy travellers can be struck down at altitude. Rest on Day 1. Rest on Day 2. The mountains will still be there on Day 3.

## 2. Know the Symptoms of Altitude Sickness

Mild AMS — a dull headache, slight fatigue — is normal in the first 24 hours and usually passes. Symptoms that demand attention: **persistent or worsening headache, nausea, dizziness, breathlessness at rest, loss of coordination, or confusion**. If these appear, do not go higher. Descend if they worsen. The nearest hospital is at Leh — SNM Hospital, operated by the Indian Army, is well-equipped for altitude emergencies.

## 3. Consider Diamox Before You Arrive

**Acetazolamide (Diamox) 250mg** helps the body acclimatise faster by stimulating deeper breathing. Start taking it **two days before arriving in Leh** — twice daily, morning and evening, with food. Continue for two days after reaching altitude. It is available on prescription in most countries. Common side effects: increased urination (drink more water to compensate) and a tingling sensation in fingers and toes. Consult your doctor before taking it, especially if you have sulfa drug allergies.

## 4. Get Your Inner Line Permits Sorted on Day 1

**Inner Line Permits (ILPs)** are required for both Indian nationals and foreigners to enter Nubra Valley, Pangong Lake, Tso Moriri, and several other border areas. Without them, you will be turned back at military checkpoints.

Apply at the **Deputy Commissioner's Office in Leh** (open Mon–Sat, 10am–1pm) or online at **lahdclehpermit.in**. Processing is usually same-day. Fees: ₹400 environment fee + ₹100 Red Cross fee + ₹150/day for the restricted area. Bring your passport (or Aadhaar for Indian nationals) and copies.

If you're with a tour operator, they will handle this for you — but confirm it explicitly when booking. Apply on Day 1 in Leh so permits are ready before you head out on Day 3.

## 5. Carry Plenty of Cash — Especially for Outside Leh

Leh has SBI and PNB ATMs. They work — usually. But they run out of cash in peak season, and they are the only ATMs you will see until you return. **Beyond Leh, Ladakh is entirely cash-only.** Nubra Valley, Pangong Lake, Turtuk, Chang La — no cards accepted anywhere. Take out more than you think you need before leaving town.

Budget roughly ₹2,000–3,000/person per day for accommodation (outside your pre-booked package), meals, and incidental costs on the road.

## 6. Don't Rely on Mobile Data Outside Leh

**Jio and BSNL** work reasonably well in Leh town. Outside it, you are largely on your own. There is no mobile signal in Nubra Valley, at Pangong Lake, or on the high mountain passes.

**Before you leave Leh each morning:** download the day's offline map on Google Maps or Maps.me, screenshot your accommodation address, and share your itinerary with someone at home. A satellite communicator (Garmin inReach or similar) is a worthwhile investment for anyone doing remote trekking.

## 7. Book Flights Early — and Always Fly in the Morning

Delhi–Leh is one of the most popular domestic routes in India in summer. **Book 2–3 months ahead** for July and August. Flight costs double or triple in peak season when booked late.

Always choose **morning departures** (before 10am). The mountains generate severe thermal turbulence by midday, leading to flight delays, diversions, and cancellations. Morning flights land before the air heats up. This isn't a preference — it's a significantly more reliable option.

## 8. Fill Up with Petrol Before Every Excursion

There are petrol pumps in Leh and a couple of checkpoints along the way to Nubra and Pangong — but **don't rely on them**. Some are military-only. Others run dry. Fill your vehicle's tank completely in Leh before heading out each day, and carry a 5-litre jerry can as backup on longer routes.

## 9. Drink 3–4 Litres of Water Per Day

Altitude and cold air dehydrate you faster than normal conditions. **Drink constantly** — small sips every 15–20 minutes rather than large amounts infrequently. Your urine should be light yellow, not dark. Avoid alcohol for the first 48 hours (it dilates blood vessels and worsens AMS symptoms), and limit caffeine.

## 10. Pack Layers for Every Possible Temperature

Even in July, Ladakh can go from 28°C at midday in Leh to 5°C at Khardung La within a 3-hour drive. At Pangong Lake overnight, even August drops to near-freezing. **Layers are the only answer.**

Non-negotiables regardless of season: down jacket, fleece mid-layer, thermal base layer, warm hat and gloves, windproof outer shell. Add SPF 50+ sunscreen (UV radiation is severe at altitude — sunburn happens in under 30 minutes) and a good pair of polarised sunglasses.

## 11. Respect the Mountain Passes — Don't Rush Them

Khardung La at 5,600m and Chang La at 5,425m are not novelties. **Spend the minimum necessary time at the summit.** Brief photo stops are fine; long lunches at the top are not. The reduced oxygen at these altitudes causes rapid fatigue and headaches. If you start feeling significantly unwell at a pass, descend immediately — the symptoms resolve quickly as you come down.

## 12. Know the Monastery Rules Before You Enter

Ladakh has more than 100 Buddhist monasteries (gompas). They are active places of worship, not museums. When visiting:
- **Remove your shoes** before entering the main prayer hall
- **Walk clockwise** around stupas and mani walls
- **Ask permission before photographing** monks or religious ceremonies — entry fees of ₹30–100 usually cover exterior photography, but interior photography may require separate permission
- **Dress modestly** — shoulders and knees covered. Carry a light scarf.

The monks are generally welcoming to respectful visitors. The early morning puja (prayer session) at Thiksey (around 6am) is one of the most moving experiences in Ladakh — arrive quietly and sit at the back.

## 13. Tip Your Driver-Guide Generously

If you're on a private tour, your driver-guide works hard — navigating high-altitude mountain roads in a vehicle packed with passengers and luggage, often starting before sunrise and finishing after dark. **₹500–800 per person as a tip at the end of the trip** is standard and genuinely appreciated. If you have a dedicated guide separate from your driver, tip both individually.

## 14. Don't Litter — Ladakh's Waste System Is Stretched

The rapid growth of Ladakh tourism has outpaced the region's waste management infrastructure. Plastic bottles, food wrappers, and non-biodegradable packaging are a serious problem on mountain roadsides, at Pangong Lake, and along trek routes. Carry your rubbish back with you. Bring a reusable water bottle and refill it from filtered sources (most guesthouses and camps provide filtered water). Buy local produce — Nubra apricots, local dried fruit — over packaged snacks when possible.

## 15. Download These Apps Before You Leave Leh

- **Google Maps (offline):** Download the Leh, Nubra, and Pangong regions for offline use before departing each day
- **Maps.me:** Works entirely offline, useful in deep valley areas
- **iNaturalist:** For identifying wildlife, plants, and birds (great for Hemis National Park)
- **SOS signal protocol:** Know the number for SNM Hospital Leh (+91 1982 252012) and save it to your phone before you lose signal

## Prepared Travellers Have the Best Trips

None of these tips are difficult to act on. Get the permits on Day 1. Carry cash. Rest those first two days. Pack layers. Drink water. Everything else is detail.

The travellers who come to Ladakh prepared are the ones who come back with the most extraordinary stories — because they spent their time in the mountains rather than dealing with avoidable problems.
