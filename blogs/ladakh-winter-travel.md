# Visiting Ladakh in Winter | What It's Really Like When the Roads Close

The photo that stops most people is the one of a lone figure walking across a frozen river, towering canyon walls rising on both sides, the ice pale blue and ancient. No roads. No vehicles. No other travellers for miles. Just ice, silence, and one of the most formidable mountain landscapes on earth.

That is Ladakh in winter. And it is not for everyone.

Here is the honest version — not a highlight reel, but a real account of what it is like to visit Ladakh between November and March: what is accessible, what is not, what the cold actually feels like, and who this journey is genuinely right for.

## What Closes in Winter

Let's start here, because it matters.

From November to late April, the **Manali–Leh Highway is closed** under snow — typically from late October through late May. The **Srinagar–Leh Highway** closes around November and reopens in April. Both of Ladakh's great overland routes become inaccessible.

That means **Leh is reachable only by air** from approximately November through mid-April. Flights from Delhi to Kushok Bakula Rimpochee Airport (LEH) continue year-round, though weather cancellations are more common in winter.

Most hotels in Leh close by mid-November and reopen in March or April. A small number of guesthouses and homestays stay open specifically for winter travellers — ask ahead and confirm.

**The Nubra Valley and Pangong Lake routes are inaccessible in winter.** Khardung La (5,600m) and Chang La (5,425m) are both snow-blocked. These destinations are simply off the table from November to April.

## What Remains — and Why It's Worth Coming

What remains is Leh itself, the monasteries of the Indus Valley, the **Chadar Trek**, and one of the world's best opportunities to spot a **snow leopard** in the wild.

### Leh Town in Winter

Leh in winter is a different city. The tourist restaurants close, the Main Bazaar empties, and the mountains — visible year-round — seem closer, larger, and more present when there is no summer dust in the air. A dusting of snow covers the flat rooftops. The Leh Palace on its hill glows in low winter sun.

A small community of year-round residents — local Ladakhis, a handful of monks, and a trickle of winter travellers — means the town is quiet but not dead. The Friday market still runs. The local dhabas serve hot Thukpa and Butter Tea. And the acclimatisation problem that bedevils summer arrivals is, oddly, less of an issue in winter: the cold air forces you to move slowly, rest more, and drink more water simply out of the need for warmth.

### The Monasteries in Winter

Summer at Thiksey Monastery means tour groups, crowds, and the constant click of cameras. January at Thiksey means you and the monks. The monastery runs on its own winter schedule — prayers, rituals, and daily life — entirely independent of the tourist calendar. If you visit in the early morning, the candlelit prayer hall with its chanting monks is one of the most quietly powerful experiences Ladakh offers.

Hemis, Spituk, and Diskit monasteries are similarly accessible in winter, and similarly empty. The monks are there. The doors are open. The experience is genuine.

## The Chadar Trek — Ladakh's Most Extreme Walk

The Chadar Trek is the reason most people come to Ladakh in winter. It is, in simple terms, a walk on the frozen Zanskar River through a 100-kilometre canyon that is otherwise completely inaccessible.

"Chadar" means blanket in Hindi — a reference to the sheet of ice that forms across the river surface when temperatures drop below –15°C in January. The local Zanskari people have walked this route for centuries, using it as their only winter connection to the outside world before roads were built. Today it is a trekking route for those willing to walk in conditions that most people would consider extreme.

**The basics:**
- **Distance:** 105km round trip (Chilling to Nerak and back)
- **Duration:** 8–10 days
- **Temperature:** –20°C to –30°C overnight, –5°C to –15°C during the day
- **Altitude:** Starts at 3,200m, relatively consistent — lower than Leh itself
- **Season:** Strictly January to mid-February. The ice must be thick enough to walk on; too early and it's dangerous, too late and it begins to crack.
- **Guide required:** Non-negotiable. The ice is dynamic — sections melt, crack, or flood overnight. An experienced Zanskari guide reads the river conditions in a way no outsider can.
- **Difficulty:** Demanding but not technically difficult in terms of climbing. The challenge is the cold, the remoteness, and the need to camp on the riverbank in sub-zero temperatures.

### What the Walking is Like

Each day covers between 8 and 15 kilometres. Some sections require scrambling over ice debris, some require wading through icy water around an impassable section of river, and some are long, meditative walks on clear blue ice with the canyon walls towering 600 metres above you.

The nights are spent in caves along the riverbank — the same caves Zanskari travellers have used for centuries. A sleeping bag rated to –30°C is not optional.

The reward at the far end of the canyon is Nerak waterfall — a 15-metre cascade frozen solid in winter, its layers of ice built up over weeks into a pale blue sculpture.

> **The Rule:** Do not attempt the Chadar Trek independently. Every year, trekkers get into serious trouble on the ice. A reputable local operator who knows the current ice conditions is not a luxury — it's the difference between a safe journey and an emergency.

## Snow Leopard Spotting in Hemis National Park

Hemis National Park, which surrounds Hemis Monastery (45km from Leh), is home to one of the highest concentrations of snow leopards on the planet. In summer, sightings are rare — the cats stay at high altitude and the park is busy.

In winter, everything changes. The snow leopards descend below 4,000 metres following the Bharal (blue sheep) they prey on, moving through terrain that is far more accessible and trackable. Specialist wildlife operators run **4–7 day snow leopard tracking expeditions** in January, February, and early March from a base near the park boundary.

Sightings are not guaranteed — this is a wild apex predator in a vast mountain landscape — but the success rate for dedicated multi-day searches in peak winter is significantly higher than most people expect. An experienced local tracker who knows the individual cat territories makes an enormous difference.

**Who runs these trips:** A small number of operators in Leh specialise in winter wildlife expeditions. Look for guides affiliated with Snow Leopard Conservancy India Trust, whose trackers work with the same animals year-round.

## What to Pack for Winter Ladakh

The cold in Ladakh is dry and still rather than damp and windchill-driven — but it is still serious cold. Pack accordingly.

**Clothing layers (non-negotiable):**
- Thermal base layer (merino wool preferred)
- Fleece mid-layer
- Down jacket rated to at least –20°C for the Chadar Trek, –10°C for Leh town
- Windproof outer shell
- Down trousers for the trek
- Balaclava, warm hat, neck gaiter
- Liner gloves + heavy insulated outer mittens
- Wool socks (multiple pairs)
- Insulated, waterproof boots rated to –20°C or below for the trek; regular winter boots for Leh

**Other essentials:**
- Hand and foot warmers (chemical heat packs)
- Power bank (cold drains batteries at 3× the normal rate — carry it inside your jacket)
- Headtorch with lithium batteries (they last in cold; alkaline ones don't)
- Lip balm SPF 50+, rich moisturiser (the dry cold chaps skin aggressively)
- Offline maps downloaded before you leave Leh (no signal in the canyon)

## Is Winter Ladakh Right for You?

Winter Ladakh is one of the most genuinely extraordinary travel experiences available in Asia. It is also demanding, expensive (flights are the only access), and requires a high tolerance for discomfort, cold, and remoteness.

If you are planning the Chadar Trek, work with a reputable local operator from the beginning — not just for safety, but because the best guides will adjust the daily route based on ice conditions that no pre-planned itinerary can predict.

If you are coming for snow leopards, book early — the best winter wildlife operators fill their January and February slots by October.

And if you are simply coming for the silence, the monasteries, and a Ladakh without tourist buses and Instagram queues — you will not be disappointed.
