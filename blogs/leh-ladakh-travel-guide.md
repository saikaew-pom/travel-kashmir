# The Leh Ladakh Travel Guide | Everything You Need to Plan Your Trip

There is a moment — somewhere between landing at Leh Airport and stepping into the thin, cold air — when you realise this is unlike anywhere else you have ever been. The mountains don't just surround you here. They swallow you whole. Brown and bone-dry, they stretch endlessly into a sky so blue it looks painted.

Ladakh sits at an average elevation of 3,500 metres above sea level. The air is lighter, the light is sharper, and the silence — once you leave the town's dusty main bazaar behind — is the kind that makes you stop walking. It's one of the most dramatic landscapes on the planet, and it rewards travellers who come prepared.

This guide covers everything you need to plan your Leh Ladakh trip properly — from how to get here and what permits you'll need, to the highlights worth going out of your way for, and the one rule that most first-timers break on day one.

## Leh Ladakh at a Glance

**Region:** Ladakh Union Territory, northern India
**Altitude:** Leh town: 3,524 m | Khardung La: 5,600 m | Pangong Lake: 4,320 m | Chang La Pass: 5,425 m
**Best time to visit:** May to September (roads fully open)
**Ideal trip length:** 7–9 days minimum
**Nearest airport:** Kushok Bakula Rimpochee Airport, Leh (LEH)
**Permits required:** Yes — Inner Line Permits for Nubra Valley and Pangong Lake

## How to Get to Leh

**By Air**
Flying into Leh is the most common and practical option for most travellers. Direct flights operate from Delhi (1 hour 20 minutes), Mumbai, and several other Indian cities. Morning flights are strongly recommended — afternoon turbulence in the mountains is notorious, and many afternoon flights get delayed or cancelled due to weather.

The landing itself is an experience. The runway at Leh sits at 3,256 metres, flanked by dramatic ridgelines on both sides. It is one of the most technically challenging approaches in Asia.

**By Road — The Manali–Leh Highway**
For travellers with more time and a taste for adventure, the overland route from Manali to Leh is one of the great road journeys in the world. The 490-kilometre drive crosses five high-altitude passes, including the Baralacha La (4,890 m) and the Tanglang La (5,328 m). It takes two days with an overnight stop at Jispa or Sarchu, and is typically open from late May to late September.

**By Road — The Srinagar–Leh Highway**
The alternative overland route comes from Srinagar in Kashmir, running 434 kilometres through the Zojila Pass (3,528 m) and past Kargil. This route offers a gentler ascent than the Manali highway, making it a slightly better option for altitude acclimatisation. It's also typically open a little earlier in the season.

> **The Rule:** Never drive from Manali to Leh in a single day, and never rush the altitude gain. Your body needs time to adjust — the roads don't.

## Best Time to Visit Ladakh

**May to June** is the shoulder season — roads open gradually from Manali in late May, the landscape is dry and stark, and accommodation is available at reasonable prices before the peak rush begins.

**July to August** is peak season. This is when the weather is warmest and most reliable, both the Manali–Leh and Srinagar–Leh highways are fully open, and Ladakh's famous festivals — including the Hemis Festival in June/July — take place. Book accommodation well in advance.

**September** is the sweet spot for many experienced travellers. The crowds thin out, the light is extraordinary, the temperatures are still manageable, and the roads remain open. Pangong Lake shimmers in the clearest skies of the year.

**October onwards**, the mountain passes begin to close and temperatures plummet. Some travellers come specifically for the silence of winter — the frozen Zanskar River trek (the Chadar Trek) runs in January and February — but it requires specialist preparation and is not a casual trip.

## The Most Important Rule in Ladakh: Acclimatisation

This is non-negotiable. Ladakh's altitude catches travellers off guard more than almost anywhere else in the world. The thin air at 3,500 metres contains roughly 40% less oxygen than at sea level. Arrive by air and that change happens in under two hours.

The Leh District Administration now mandates a strict 48-hour rest period for all travellers arriving by air. **No high-altitude excursions — including Khardung La Pass — are permitted on the first two days.** This isn't a suggestion. Ignoring it puts you at genuine risk.

**What to do those first two days:**
- Rest at your hotel. Genuinely rest. Light walks around Leh town are fine; strenuous activity is not.
- Drink 3–4 litres of water per day. Small sips, often.
- Avoid alcohol completely for the first 48 hours.
- Consider Diamox (acetazolamide) 250mg — taken twice daily, starting two days before arrival and continued for two days after reaching Leh — which helps the body process altitude faster. Consult your doctor before taking it.

**Symptoms to watch for:** persistent headache, nausea, dizziness, loss of appetite, and breathlessness at rest. Any of these beyond mild discomfort means slow down and descend if necessary.

The good news: if you give yourself two days in Leh first, most travellers adjust well and enjoy the rest of the trip without significant problems.

## The Highlights: What Not to Miss

### Leh Town (Days 1–2)
Start here, slowly. The **Leh Palace** — a nine-storey fortress built in 1630 and modelled after the Potala Palace in Tibet — rises above the old quarter and gives you an immediate sense of the region's layered history. The **Shanti Stupa**, perched on Chanspa Hill, offers 360-degree panoramic views of the town and is one of the finest spots for both sunrise and sunset. Walk through the **Leh Main Market** in the evening — it's small, manageable, and full of Ladakhi handicrafts, warm shawls, and local restaurants.

On Day 2, explore the surroundings: the **Hall of Fame** museum (run by the Indian Army, entry ₹200) tells the story of the region's military history, **Spituk Gompa** sits 8 kilometres south of Leh, and the famous **Magnetic Hill** — 30 kilometres out on the Leh–Kargil highway — is the spot where vehicles appear to roll uphill on their own. Nearby, the **Sangam Viewpoint** reveals the vivid confluence of the turquoise Zanskar and the brown Indus River.

### Khardung La Pass → Nubra Valley (Day 3)
The drive from Leh to Nubra Valley via Khardung La is the journey that defines most Ladakh itineraries. Khardung La sits at 5,600 metres — one of the highest motorable roads in the world — and the views from its summit across the Karakoram Range into Pakistan are staggering. The 160-kilometre drive to Nubra takes around 4–5 hours.

Nubra Valley translates to "Valley of Flowers." It is also, improbably, a desert. The **Silver Sand Dunes** near Hunder are where you'll find the surreal sight of Bactrian (double-humped) camels wandering across a high-altitude sandscape surrounded by snow-capped peaks. A short camel ride costs around ₹300 (20 minutes) and is entirely worth it for the photograph alone.

### Turtuk Village (Day 4)
An 80-kilometre drive from Nubra, Turtuk is one of Ladakh's most quietly extraordinary places. This remote village — only opened to tourists in 2009 — sits right on the border with Pakistan and was, until 1971, part of Pakistani-administered territory. The people here are Balti, Muslim, and speak their own language. The village grows apricots and apples, moves at its own pace, and feels utterly removed from the rest of the world. It is 3 hours each way, but travellers who make the effort consistently call it the highlight of their trip.

### Diskit Monastery → Pangong Lake (Day 5)
**Diskit Monastery** (3,144 metres), the oldest and largest in Nubra, was established in 1963. Its giant statue of Maitreya Buddha gazes protectively across the valley. From Diskit, the route south to Pangong Lake is a 4–5 hour drive through some of the most dramatic mountain scenery in Ladakh.

**Pangong Lake** stops you in your tracks the moment it comes into view. The lake stretches 134 kilometres long and 5 kilometres wide across the India–China border — 75% of it lies in Tibet. At 4,320 metres above sea level, it is the highest saltwater lake in the world. The water shifts colour through the day — turquoise, sapphire, silver — but the deep blue of late afternoon, when the light is low and the surface seems to glow, is what Pangong does to you. Stay overnight in one of the lakeside camps. The stars here are implausible.

### Chang La Pass → Thiksey Monastery → Shey Palace (Day 6)
The return from Pangong to Leh crosses **Chang La Pass** at 5,425 metres — the third-highest motorable road in the world. En route, stop at **Thiksey Monastery**, 19 kilometres east of Leh. Inspired by the Potala Palace in Lhasa, Thiksey is a 12-storey complex housing monks, prayer halls, and a commanding view of the Indus Valley. It's the second-largest monastery in Ladakh after Hemis. Nearby, **Shey Palace** — the former summer palace of the Ladakhi kings — houses the largest copper-and-gold statue of Shakyamuni Buddha in the region.

## Permits You Will Need

**Inner Line Permits (ILP)** are required for foreign nationals and Indian citizens to enter restricted border areas including Nubra Valley and Pangong Lake.

- ILPs can be obtained in Leh at the Deputy Commissioner's Office or online via the LAHDC portal before departure.
- **Fees:** Environment Fee ₹400 + Red Cross Fee ₹100 + daily permit charges per area.
- Your accommodation in Leh can typically arrange this for you — your passports will be collected on Day 1 and returned once the permits are processed.

If you're booking through a tour operator, confirm that permits are included. They should be.

## Practical Tips Before You Go

**Packing essentials:** Layers are everything. Even in summer, temperatures drop sharply at night and at altitude. Bring a down jacket, thermal base layers, a good sun hat, high-SPF sunscreen (the UV intensity at altitude is severe), and a reusable water bottle. Wet wipes are genuinely useful — particularly on the road to Nubra and Pangong where public facilities are basic.

**Connectivity:** Mobile data in Leh town (Jio and BSNL work best) is functional but patchy. Outside Leh, don't expect it. Download offline maps before you leave, and let people at home know your itinerary.

**Budget:** A well-organised 7-day private trip, including accommodation, all transport, and permits (excluding international flights), runs approximately ₹35,000–55,000 per person depending on group size. Lunch is not included in most tour packages — expect to spend ₹200–400 per meal at restaurants along the route.

**Tipping:** Local guides and drivers who accompany you throughout work hard at altitude. A tip of ₹500–800 per person for your driver-guide at the end of the trip is standard and appreciated.

**Money:** Carry Indian Rupees in cash before leaving Leh. ATMs exist in town but are unreliable, and Nubra and Pangong are entirely cash-only.
