# How To Get From Leh to Nubra Valley via Khardung La

At 5,600 metres above sea level, Khardung La is one of the highest motorable roads on the planet. The air is thin. The views are vast. The road drops away on both sides with no guardrails. And beyond the pass, the Shyok and Nubra rivers carve a green valley through the Karakoram that looks like it belongs on a different planet from the barren plateau below.

This is the most iconic drive in Ladakh. Here is exactly how to do it.

## The Route: Leh to Nubra Valley, Kilometre by Kilometre

**Total distance:** 160km, Leh to Hunder
**Total drive time:** 4–5 hours (depending on Khardung La stop)

Here is what you pass through and when:

**Leh (3,524m) → South Pullu checkpost (3,800m):** 25km, 45 minutes. The road climbs steadily north out of Leh. South Pullu is the permit checkpoint — have your Inner Line Permit ready. You will stop here and wait a few minutes while the officer records your details.

**South Pullu → Khardung La summit (5,600m):** 14km, 45–60 minutes. The road becomes narrower and more dramatic. The last 5km are steep switchbacks. The surface is patchy — some newly resurfaced sections, some broken and rocky near the top. This section is not fast. Keep your speed low and your line to the left.

**Khardung La summit:** Stop for 20–30 minutes maximum. Tea, photographs, acknowledge the altitude. Then descend.

**Khardung La → North Pullu checkpost (3,200m):** 25km, 45 minutes. The descent is steep and the road quality varies. Your driver will take this slow. You'll start to feel the air thicken noticeably as you drop altitude — a welcome sensation.

**North Pullu → Khalsar (3,100m):** 50km, 1 hour. The road flattens and the valley opens up. You're in the Shyok Valley now. The landscape changes completely — green fields, willow trees, the turquoise Shyok River running alongside the road. This is the valley that surprises everyone.

**Khalsar → Diskit (3,072m):** 20km, 30 minutes. Diskit is the main town of Nubra Valley, the administrative centre, and your base for the next two days.

**Diskit → Hunder (3,150m):** 10km, 20 minutes. Hunder is the sand dunes area — the part of Nubra that most people are coming to see. The majority of accommodation is here.

## Step 1: Get Your Inner Line Permit

You need an Inner Line Permit to enter Nubra Valley. No exceptions.

**What it costs:**
- Environment fee: ₹400
- Red Cross fee: ₹100
- Applicable per person per visit

**Where to get it:**

Option A — DC Office, Leh: Near Polo Ground. Open Monday to Saturday, 10am to 1pm. Queue early, especially in peak season. Bring your passport (foreigners) or Aadhaar (Indian nationals). Processing takes 30–60 minutes.

Option B — Online: lahdclehpermit.in. Upload your ID, fill the form, pay. Print it or keep it on your phone. The checkpost at South Pullu accepts digital copies.

**When to get it:** Get your permit the evening before your departure, or the morning of the day before. Do not try to get it on the morning you want to drive. The DC Office opens at 10am and your taxi should leave by 7–8am to make the most of the day.

## Step 2: Book Your Taxi

Private taxi from Leh to Nubra and back: ₹6,500–8,500 for a 2-day trip (Leh → Nubra on Day 1, Nubra → Leh on Day 2/3). Arrange at Leh Main Taxi Stand or through your guesthouse.

Book the evening before. Tell the driver your exact itinerary: departure time, stops you want to make at Khardung La and on the road, which village you're staying in, and what time you want to start Day 2 sightseeing.

Agree: is fuel included? Is the driver's accommodation covered in the rate (standard practice is you cover ₹300–500/night for the driver)? What stops are included?

For a 2-night stay in Nubra, a 3-day taxi hire works out to ₹9,000–12,000 total — negotiate a day rate that accounts for the longer engagement.

## Step 3: Khardung La — What to Expect and How to Handle the Altitude

Khardung La is at 5,600m. That is higher than Everest Base Camp. It is higher than any peak in the Alps, the Rockies, or the Andes. The comparison is not made for drama — it is to help you take the altitude seriously.

**What you'll see at the top:**

A cluster of army tea stalls selling hot chai and Maggi noodles. A large signboard declaring "Highest Motorable Road" (technically disputed, but the claim is genuine in spirit). Prayer flags in every colour running across the road. Mountains in every direction, many of them over 6,000m.

The view, on a clear day, is extraordinary. The valley of Leh is visible to the south. The Karakoram range extends to the north. The horizon is simply enormous.

**How to behave at 5,600m:**

- Walk slowly. Do not jog to the sign for a photo.
- Drink hot tea. It helps.
- Sit down if you feel dizzy. Tell your driver.
- 20–30 minutes is the right amount of time. Do not linger for an hour.
- Do not sleep at the pass. Get back in the vehicle and descend.

Symptoms of altitude sickness — severe headache, nausea, loss of coordination — mean go down immediately. Altitude illness resolves rapidly with descent. It does not resolve by toughing it out at altitude.

**Who should not attempt Khardung La:**

Anyone with heart or respiratory conditions should consult a doctor before this trip. Children under 8 are generally fine but watch them carefully. If you haven't yet spent 2 full nights in Leh acclimatising, reconsider your departure date — Khardung La on day one after flying is a bad combination.

## Step 4: Road Conditions on the Khardung La Route

The road has been improved in recent years but remains genuinely challenging in sections.

**Leh to South Pullu:** Good. Newly surfaced, wide, straightforward.

**South Pullu to Khardung La summit:** Narrow, switchbacks, some loose gravel near the top. No guardrails on significant sections. The road is wide enough for two vehicles but requires courtesy — army trucks and tankers use this road constantly. When you meet one, pull left, stop, and let it pass.

**Khardung La to North Pullu:** Steep descent with tight corners. Your driver will take this slowly. This is not the section for conversation — let the driver concentrate.

**North Pullu to Diskit:** Mostly good. The Shyok Valley road is wide and well-maintained since the border road construction push in recent years. Some sections are gravel but nothing technical.

**Practical notes:**
- Fill your petrol tank in Leh before departure. There is a petrol pump in Diskit (Nubra Valley) but it can run dry in peak season. Do not rely on it.
- Carry ₹4,000–5,000 cash minimum per person. Nubra is entirely cash-only. No ATMs.
- Download offline Google Maps for the Nubra Valley area before leaving Leh. There is no mobile signal from South Pullu to Khalsar.

## Step 5: What to Do in Nubra Valley

### Diskit Monastery

Diskit Gompa is the oldest and largest monastery in Nubra Valley, dating to the 14th century. It sits high on a rocky promontory above the valley floor. The walk up takes 15 minutes from the car park and the views from the top — over the green Nubra Valley, the Shyok River, and the surrounding Karakoram — are worth every step.

The giant Maitreya Buddha statue at the monastery entrance was completed in 2010. It is 32 metres tall and visible from most of the valley.

Best time: morning or late afternoon for the best light.

**Entry:** ₹30 per person.

### Hunder Sand Dunes

The sand dunes of Hunder are one of those geographical anomalies that Ladakh specialises in. At 3,150m in the middle of the Himalayas, there are actual sand dunes — high, soft, wind-sculpted — surrounded by snow-capped peaks. The effect is surreal and genuinely beautiful.

The dunes are most photogenic in the golden hour before sunset and in the early morning before buses of day-trippers arrive from Leh.

**Bactrian Camel rides:** Double-humped Bactrian camels are found here — they were once part of the Silk Road trading caravans. A short camel ride (30–45 minutes) costs ₹500–800 per person and is absolutely worth doing, particularly with children.

### Turtuk Village

This is the one that surprises people. Turtuk is the last village before the Line of Control with Pakistan — it only opened to tourists in 2010, having been part of Pakistan until 1971. It is 80km north of Diskit on a beautiful river road.

The village is Balti-speaking, its architecture distinctly Central Asian — flat-roofed stone houses, apricot orchards, rose gardens growing against the stone walls. The apricots ripen in July and August and are the best you will ever eat.

The drive from Hunder to Turtuk takes about 2 hours. It is worth doing if you have the time — plan it as a full-day excursion on Day 2 in Nubra.

**Important:** Turtuk requires an additional permit, usually arranged alongside your Nubra permit at the DC Office. Ask specifically for Turtuk when you apply.

### Panamik Hot Springs

55km north of Diskit, Panamik has natural hot springs that are popular with army personnel and a growing number of tourists. After a day of driving, a soak at a natural hot spring in the Karakoram is a memorable experience. Basic changing facilities available.

## The 2-Night Nubra Itinerary

**Day 1 — Leh to Nubra via Khardung La:**
- 7:00am: Depart Leh. Stop for views and tea at Khardung La.
- 12:30pm: Arrive Diskit. Lunch.
- 2:30pm: Diskit Monastery.
- 5:00pm: Hunder Sand Dunes. Sunset from the dunes.
- 7:00pm: Check in to camp/guesthouse in Hunder.

**Day 2 — Turtuk excursion or Panamik:**
- 7:30am: Breakfast and morning walk to the dunes (before crowds).
- 9:00am: Depart for Turtuk (if Day 2 plan) or Panamik hot springs.
- 1:00pm: Lunch in Turtuk or on the road.
- 3:30pm: Return drive toward Hunder/Diskit.
- 6:00pm: Final evening in the valley. Dunes at sunset again (never gets old).

**Day 3 — Nubra to Leh:**
- Option A: Return to Leh via Khardung La (same route, 4–5 hours).
- Option B: Continue to Pangong Lake via the Shyok Valley road (150km, 5–6 hours, no Khardung La crossing needed). This is a spectacular circuit if you're continuing your trip.

## Where to Stay in Nubra

**Hunder:** The most popular base. Close to the dunes. Range of camps and guesthouses from budget to comfortable.

**Diskit:** The main town. More guesthouses and small hotels. Slightly less atmospheric than Hunder but more practical for those going to Turtuk.

**Sumur:** Quieter, fewer tourists, good guesthouses. Good choice for a second night if you want variety.

Book in advance for July and August — Nubra fills up in peak season. Your guesthouse in Leh can usually arrange this with a phone call.
