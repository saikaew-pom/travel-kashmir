# How To Get From Leh to Pangong Lake | Route, Permits & Tips

The photographs don't prepare you for the real thing. That improbable blue — impossibly bright, shifting from turquoise to cobalt to deep navy depending on cloud and hour — set against bare brown mountains and a thin strip of sky. Pangong Tso is one of those places that actually looks better in real life than in pictures.

Getting there from Leh takes about five hours each way. The route crosses Chang La Pass at 5,425m. The logistics involve permits, cash, and a decision about whether to stay overnight (you should). This guide walks you through everything.

## Step 1: Get Your Permit the Day Before

You cannot go to Pangong without an Inner Line Permit. This is a hard requirement — checkposts on the road will turn you back without it.

**What it costs:**
- Environment fee: ₹400
- Red Cross fee: ₹100
- Wildlife fee (for some zones): ₹150 per day

Total: approximately ₹650–700 per person per visit.

**Where to get it:**

Option A — DC Office, Leh: Deputy Commissioner's Office, near Polo Ground. Open Monday to Saturday, 10am to 1pm. Queue early. Bring your passport (foreigners) or Aadhaar card (Indian nationals). Permits are usually issued same day, within an hour.

Option B — Online: lahdclehpermit.in. Available 24 hours. Upload a scan of your ID document, fill in the form, pay online. Print the permit or keep it on your phone. Most checkposts accept a digital copy.

**The practical rule:** Get your permit the evening you arrive in Leh, or the morning of the day before your Pangong trip. Do not try to get it on the morning you're planning to leave. Queues, technical issues, or a closed office will destroy your early start.

**One important note:** Permits are valid for a specific date range. Make sure the dates on your permit cover both the day you arrive at Pangong and the day you return. If you're doing one night, get a 2-day permit.

## Step 2: Choose Your Route

There are two ways to drive from Leh to Pangong Tso.

### Via Chang La Pass (Recommended)
Distance: 160km. Time: approximately 5 hours.

This is the standard tourist route and the one worth taking. The road heads east from Leh on NH1 to Karu, then turns south toward Chang La. The pass sits at 5,425m — one of the highest motorable passes in the world. There is an army checkpoint at the summit, a tea stall, and on clear days, views that extend for hundreds of kilometres.

After the pass, the road descends through Durbuk and eventually reaches Spangmik, the main village on the Indian shore of Pangong.

### Via Karu on NH1 (Slightly Shorter)
Distance: 155km. Time: roughly the same.

This route rejoins the Chang La route at Durbuk and then continues to the lake. It is marginally shorter but bypasses the pass. Unless you've already crossed Chang La and want a variation for the return, stick to the Chang La route on the way in.

## Step 3: Understand Chang La Pass

Chang La is not just a high point on the map. At 5,425m, it is higher than most trekking peaks in Nepal and Europe. The air is thin. The pass is cold even in July — pack a jacket regardless of the date.

**What the pass is like:**

The road approaching from the Leh side is reasonably well-maintained, thanks to the army's heavy use of this route. The summit has a small shrine, a tea stall run by the army (hot tea, Maggi noodles), and a board declaring it the "Third Highest Motorable Pass in the World." There are prayer flags everywhere.

The descent toward Pangong is more dramatic — steep switchbacks, occasional loose gravel, views opening up over the valley below.

**Altitude precautions at Chang La:**

Stop briefly. Drink tea. Don't run around trying to get the perfect photograph. Altitude sickness at 5,425m is a real risk — shortness of breath is normal, but dizziness or a bad headache means sit down, breathe slowly, and tell your driver. Most drivers on this route have seen it before and know what to do.

The standard advice: spend no more than 20–30 minutes at the summit. Get back in the vehicle and descend. You will feel better at lower altitude within minutes.

**Before you leave Leh:**
- Eat breakfast. A proper one.
- Carry 3–4 litres of water per person. There are no reliable water sources between Leh and Spangmik.
- Bring layers. Temperature at Chang La can be 0–5°C even in summer.
- Carry cash. The lake area is entirely cash-only.
- Charge your phone and camera and download offline maps before leaving — there is no signal for most of the journey.

## Step 4: Overnight or Day Trip?

This is the most important decision you'll make about this trip. Here is the honest answer: **do the overnight.**

**Why a day trip fails:**

The drive from Leh to Pangong takes roughly 5 hours. The drive back takes another 5 hours. Add in the Chang La stop and time at the lake, and a day trip means approximately 10 hours of driving for 1.5 to 2 hours actually at the lake. You'll arrive in the early afternoon when the light is flat and the tourist crowds are at their peak. You'll have to leave before sunset to get back to Leh before dark (driving mountain passes at night is not a good idea). You will have spent a very long day in a vehicle and seen the lake in its least spectacular conditions.

**Why overnight is worth it:**

Pangong at dawn is one of the great sights in the Himalayas. The lake turns pink and gold in the first light. The water is completely still. There are almost no other visitors out before 7am. If you stay the night, this scene belongs to you.

Sunset the evening before is nearly as good. The light rakes across the mountains at a low angle, and the lake surface mirrors it exactly.

A full evening at the lake after the day-trippers leave is genuinely peaceful. The silence at 4,350m with no light pollution and a sky full of stars is something that stays with you.

## Step 5: Where to Stay at Pangong

The main accommodation hub on the Indian side of Pangong is **Spangmik village**, approximately 7km from the Pangong checkpost. The main settlement sits right on the lake shore.

**Accommodation options:**

- **Tented camps:** The standard and the most popular. Semi-permanent canvas tents with attached bathrooms (basic but functional). Range from simple budget camps to more comfortable "luxury" options. Price: ₹2,500–6,000 per night including dinner and breakfast.
- **Guesthouses:** A handful of family-run guesthouses in Spangmik village. Simpler, cheaper (₹1,200–2,000/night), and a slightly more authentic experience. You eat whatever the family is cooking.

**What to expect at all options:**
- No electricity beyond solar — charge everything in Leh before you leave.
- No mobile signal. This is a good thing.
- Cold nights. Even in July, temperatures drop to 5–10°C. Good sleeping bags are provided at most camps but confirm when booking.
- Very basic bathrooms. Hot water is usually available in the mornings only.
- The food is simple — dal, rice, vegetables, noodles, eggs. Good enough. Do not expect restaurant variety.

**Booking:** Your taxi driver can usually call ahead and arrange a camp. Alternatively, ask your guesthouse in Leh to book on your behalf. In peak season (July–August), do not arrive without a booking — Spangmik fills up.

## Step 6: What to Bring

Pangong is not the place to discover you've forgotten something.

- **Layers:** Morning and evening temperatures at 4,350m can be 5–10°C even in summer. Midday can hit 20–25°C. Dress in layers you can add or remove.
- **Sun protection:** The UV intensity at high altitude is extreme. Sunscreen SPF 50+, sunglasses (polarised are worth it near reflective water), lip balm, and a hat.
- **Cash:** There are no ATMs at Pangong or anywhere between Durbuk and Spangmik. The camp fees, any bottled water, and souvenirs are all cash. Carry at least ₹3,000–4,000 per person.
- **Power bank:** Your phone is your camera and your offline map. A full-charged power bank is essential.
- **Offline maps:** Download this area in Google Maps before leaving Leh. There is zero phone signal on the route and at the lake.
- **Snacks:** The drive is long and the road passes no shops between Leh and Durbuk. Carry biscuits, nuts, and chocolate.

## The Dawn Light

Set an alarm for 5:30am on your morning at Pangong.

Walk down to the water before anyone else is awake. The light arrives slowly — first a pale grey, then a wash of pink on the mountains to the east, then the gold. The lake absorbs and reflects all of it. At that hour, the air is absolutely still and the water is a mirror.

This is why you stayed the night.
