# Trekking the Markha Valley | Ladakh's Most Beloved Trek Route

The first thing they tell you in Leh is: you need to acclimatise before you trek. The second thing they tell you is: if you only do one trek in Ladakh, do Markha Valley.

Both pieces of advice are correct.

Ladakh has dozens of routes. Some are harder. Some go higher. Some are wilder and more remote. But Markha Valley has something the others tend to lack — a complete experience. You walk through three distinct ecological zones. You pass ancient monasteries. You cross a 5,200-metre pass with views that include the Karakoram to the north and the Zanskar Range to the south. You sleep in farmhouses where the family serves you dinner and then sits with you by the stove while the wind moves outside.

It is achievable for a fit traveller with no technical climbing skills. It is spectacular enough that experienced trekkers come back to do it a second time. And it runs through Hemis National Park — the largest national park in India — the last stronghold of the snow leopard in the Himalayan region.

## The Route at a Glance

The Markha Valley Trek is typically walked over five to six days, covering approximately 70 kilometres. The classic version starts at Chilling (or nearby Spituk, near Leh) and ends at Hemis Monastery, or in reverse.

The most popular direction is Chilling to Hemis:

- **Chilling → Skiu** (Day 1)
- **Skiu → Markha village** (Day 2–3)
- **Markha → Hankar** (Day 3–4)
- **Hankar → Nimaling** (Day 4–5)
- **Nimaling → Shang Sumdo via Kongmaru La** (Day 5–6)
- **Shang Sumdo → Hemis** (Day 6)

Total altitude gain over the route: from roughly 3,400 metres at Chilling to 5,200 metres at Kongmaru La. The route then descends steeply to Hemis at around 3,600 metres.

Many operators now offer the reverse direction — Hemis to Chilling — which means you cross Kongmaru La early in the trek when legs are fresher but acclimatisation is still building. Both directions work. Chilling to Hemis is the more gradual approach to altitude, which is why most guides recommend it for trekkers new to Himalayan altitudes.

## Day-by-Day Route Summary

### Day 1 — Chilling to Skiu (14km, ~5 hours)

The trail begins with a river crossing. At Chilling, a hand-operated cable trolley — a metal box suspended on a wire above the Zanskar River — carries you across. It is both alarming and strangely satisfying.

From the far bank, the path follows the Markha River upstream, gaining altitude gradually. The landscape is open and spare. Rock formations, river willows, the occasional herd of goats above you on a hillside. The village of Skiu appears after five hours of walking — a cluster of stone houses, a small monastery on the hill above, and the first homestay of the trip.

### Day 2 — Skiu to Markha (19km, ~6 hours)

The longest day in terms of distance. The valley opens and narrows repeatedly as you walk. You'll pass through the villages of Sara and Markha Sumdo. The trail crosses the Markha River multiple times — in June and July these crossings can be knee-deep and cold; by August the river is usually lower.

Towards the end of the day, Kang Yatse — a dramatic twin-peaked massif at 6,400 metres — comes into view ahead. Its appearance on the horizon signals that you're properly in the valley now.

Markha village, where you spend the night, sits at around 3,700 metres. It has the highest concentration of homestays on the route, a small monastery, a school, and a population of perhaps 30 families.

### Day 3 — Markha to Hankar (12km, ~4 hours)

A shorter walking day, which is useful because the altitude is increasing. The trail passes the ruins of Markha Fort on a rocky spur above the river — worth the short detour up for the views. Beyond Markha, the valley narrows and steepens.

Hankar sits at roughly 4,100 metres. The village is small — a handful of houses. Above it, on an improbable rock tower, the ruins of Hankar Fort and monastery cling to the summit. The evening here, with the mountains closing in and the sky going dark, is one of the great camping moments of the trek.

### Day 4 — Hankar to Nimaling (13km, ~5 hours)

The trail leaves the main valley and climbs toward the high plateau. By now the vegetation has thinned — grass and scree instead of the river willows and wild rose of the lower valley.

Nimaling is a seasonal high-altitude pasture at 4,700 metres, used by Changpa nomads in summer as grazing ground for their pashmina goats. In trekking season, a small cluster of tents serves as a camp. There are no permanent structures. The Kang Yatse massif looms directly overhead. At this altitude, the cold sets in by early afternoon. Drink plenty of water and rest.

### Day 5 — Nimaling to Shang Sumdo via Kongmaru La (14km, ~6–7 hours)

This is the day. Kongmaru La at 5,200 metres is the high point of the entire trek, and crossing it is an experience that stays with travellers for years.

The ascent from Nimaling takes two to three hours. The final section is on loose shale — slow, deliberate, breathing measured. At the pass, the view opens in every direction. To the north: the Indus Valley, the Karakoram Range beyond it. To the south: the Zanskar peaks. The Himalayas as a 360-degree panorama.

Take your time at the top. Add a prayer flag to the pile if you've brought one. Then begins the long descent — loose trail dropping steeply through increasingly green landscape toward the Indus Valley. Your knees will feel it. The village of Shang Sumdo appears at the bottom — a few houses, a small tea shop, and road access.

### Day 6 — Shang Sumdo to Hemis (6km, ~2 hours)

A gentle final walk along the valley floor to Hemis Monastery — the largest monastery in Ladakh, home to some of the region's most important thangka paintings and the famous Hemis Festival held each summer. Spend an hour in the monastery complex before arranging transport back to Leh, 45 kilometres away.

## Best Season

The Markha Valley Trek is done between June and September.

**June** is early season — the river crossings are at their highest from snowmelt, and some of the higher trails may still have snow. The landscape is at its most dramatic, with the greatest contrast between river green and mountain brown. Fewer trekkers. Lower temperatures at night.

**July and August** are peak season. The crossings are more manageable, the weather is most stable, and the nomad camps at Nimaling are occupied. This is the best time for first-timers.

**September** brings cooler temperatures, even lower rivers, and noticeably fewer people on the trail. The light is softer, the air clearer, and the first hints of autumn colour appear in the lower villages. Many experienced trekkers consider September the best month.

October is possible but the higher sections are cold and early snowfall can close Kongmaru La unexpectedly. Don't plan a late October crossing without local advice.

## Guided or Independent?

Both are viable. The question depends on your experience level and what you want from the trek.

**Guided trekking** with a reputable Leh-based operator is the easier and safer option for most travellers. A basic guided package — guide, horsemen to carry camping equipment and food, tents, meals — runs approximately ₹3,500–5,000 per person per day depending on group size. This takes all logistics off your hands, provides local expertise and knowledge, and supports the local economy directly.

**Independent trekking** is possible. You can hire a local guide-cum-horseman in Chilling at the start of the route. The trail is generally well-marked, though it can be unclear in a few places. You can eat and sleep entirely in homestays, eliminating the need to carry camping equipment.

For the independent route, register on the eGramSwaraj portal to get your trekking permit, or register at the trail head. Carry ₹50–100 per person for the Hemis National Park entrance fee.

## What to Pack

The Markha Valley Trek is not a technical climb, but it is a serious high-altitude undertaking that reaches 5,200 metres. Under-packing is the main mistake first-time Himalayan trekkers make.

**Footwear:** Broken-in hiking boots with ankle support. Trail running shoes work for experienced trekkers but offer less protection on the river crossings and rocky descents. Lightweight camp shoes or sandals for evenings.

**Layers:** Temperature swings are extreme. At Nimaling in August, days can reach 18°C; nights drop to near zero. Bring a base layer, a mid-layer fleece, and a proper down jacket or equivalent insulated layer. Waterproof shell for rain.

**River crossings:** Trekking poles are invaluable for stability. Quick-dry material for lower half — you will get wet. A dry bag for phone, documents, and anything electronic.

**Health:** Diamox (acetazolamide) for altitude — consult a doctor before your trek. Basic first aid kit. Lip balm and high-SPF sunscreen (UV radiation at altitude is intense). Iodine tablets or a water filter. Electrolytes.

**Documents:** Carry your Inner Line Permit if your route passes through restricted areas. Your passport. Emergency contacts in Leh.

## Homestays and the Culture of the Valley

The homestay culture of Markha Valley is one of its greatest pleasures.

These are not guest houses converted for tourists. They are working farms that open their rooms and their kitchens to trekkers for a modest fee — typically ₹500–800 per night including dinner and breakfast. You sleep on a thick Ladakhi mattress rolled out on the floor of a spare room. Dinner is served at a communal table: dal, rice, tsampa (roasted barley flour), sometimes vegetables from the kitchen garden.

In Markha village, several families have been hosting trekkers for twenty years. They know every face that appears at their door has just walked a long day. The food comes hot. The butter tea comes without asking.

These interactions are not a performance. They are hospitality in its original sense — strangers arriving tired, being fed and warmed, continuing in the morning. Don't rush dinner. Ask about the farm. Learn three words of Ladakhi before you start the trek. You will be received differently.

## Wildlife in Hemis National Park

The Markha Valley passes through Hemis National Park, India's largest national park by area. It is the most important snow leopard habitat in the Indian subcontinent — with an estimated 200–400 individuals in the broader region.

You are unlikely to see a snow leopard on a summer trek. They move to higher ground in the warm months. Winter is the time to see them — organised snow leopard tracking expeditions in January and February take place in the Rumbak Valley near Hemis, just outside the Markha Valley route. But you are walking through their territory, and the knowledge of that adds something to the experience.

What you will almost certainly see: **Bharal blue sheep** — stocky, blue-grey ungulates that graze in herds on the rocky slopes above the trail. They are a snow leopard's primary prey animal, and watching a herd of fifty scramble effortlessly up a near-vertical scree slope puts your own laboured trudging toward Kongmaru La in context.

Look also for **lammergeier** (bearded vulture), golden eagles, and the occasional red fox. The valley is alive with birds in summer.

## The Trek Worth Every Step

There is a moment on Kongmaru La that is hard to describe without sounding like you're overselling it. You've been walking for four days. Your legs are tired in the specific way that only sustained uphill at altitude creates. The shale is loose underfoot and your breathing is laboured. You take the last fifty steps slowly.

And then you're there. All of it, in every direction.

The Markha Valley Trek gives you this moment. It earns it for you, step by step, over five or six days of river crossings and monastery visits and dinner with farming families and nights cold enough to see your breath inside your sleeping bag.

This is not the most extreme trek in Ladakh. But it is, for most travellers, the most complete one.
