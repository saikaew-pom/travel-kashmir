# A Guide to Nubra Valley | Ladakh's Sand Dunes, Camels and Monasteries

You spend your first two days in Leh at 3,524 metres, acclimatising, drinking sweet butter tea, sleeping more than you've slept in years. You look out of your guesthouse window and see brown mountains. More brown mountains.

Then someone tells you there's a valley a few hours north where double-humped camels roam silver sand dunes against a backdrop of snow-capped Himalayan peaks.

You don't quite believe it. You go anyway. And it turns out to be even more extraordinary than described.

Nubra Valley sits at around 3,000 metres — lower than Leh — which is part of what makes it feel like another world. The air is slightly thicker. Orchards grow here. Apricot trees line the roadsides. The Shyok and Nubra rivers carve through a wide, flat valley floor that was once the bed of a glacial lake. And at Hunder, inexplicably, there are sand dunes.

This is your guide to making the most of Nubra.

## Getting There: The Road Over Khardung La

The drive from Leh to Nubra Valley is 160 kilometres and takes four to five hours. It sounds straightforward. It is anything but.

The route crosses Khardung La Pass at 5,600 metres — one of the highest motorable roads on earth. The ascent is dramatic. The descent into Nubra is even more so: hairpin after hairpin, the valley floor materialising below you in bands of green and gold, the rivers glinting silver in the distance.

A few things to know before you drive this road. You cannot go until Day 3 after flying into Leh — the 48-hour acclimatisation rule is non-negotiable, and for good reason. Altitude sickness at Khardung La is a real risk if your body hasn't had time to adjust. Don't rush it.

The road is open from roughly late May through late September, depending on snowfall. Hire a driver in Leh — most guesthouses can arrange a Jeep (typically a Mahindra Scorpio or Innova) for around ₹3,500–5,000 per day including fuel. Shared cabs are also available from Leh's main taxi stand if you're travelling solo and want to keep costs down.

Start early. Aim to be at Khardung La by mid-morning before the wind picks up. Stop, take the obligatory photograph at the summit signboard, drink the tea sold by the stall there, and carry on — the pass is not a place to linger.

## The Inner Line Permit

All visitors to Nubra Valley — Indian nationals and foreigners alike — need an Inner Line Permit (ILP). This is not optional and checkposts do verify it.

There are two ways to get one. The first is the Deputy Commissioner's Office in Leh (on Fort Road, near the main market). Bring photocopies of your passport or ID, your visa if you're a foreign national, and passport-sized photos. It usually takes an hour or less. The second option is the LAHDC (Ladakh Autonomous Hill Development Council) online portal, which allows you to apply before you arrive.

The cost is modest: ₹400 as an environment fee plus ₹100 for the Red Cross fee, plus small daily charges. Get the permit for Pangong at the same time if you're heading there afterward — it saves a trip.

## What to See in Nubra Valley

### Hunder: Sand Dunes and Bactrian Camels

The dunes at Hunder are the thing most people come to Nubra for, and they live up to the hype. These are not the dunes of Rajasthan — they're smaller, stranger, more unexpected. What makes them remarkable is the context: you're standing at altitude in a Himalayan valley with river willows on one side and snowfields on the other, and somehow there are sand dunes.

The camels here are Bactrian — double-humped, the Central Asian variety that once plied the ancient Silk Road trade routes through this exact valley. A short camel ride costs around ₹300 for 20 minutes. It is, frankly, delightful.

The best time to visit the dunes is late afternoon, when the light turns the sand copper and the mountains behind go pink. Go early morning if you want them to yourself.

### Diskit Monastery

Diskit is Nubra's main town, five kilometres from Hunder. Above it, on a ridge, sits Diskit Monastery — the oldest in Nubra Valley, established in the 14th century and home to the oldest Gelugpa order in the region.

What dominates the view, though, is the massive Maitreya Buddha statue erected in 1999 at the instruction of the Dalai Lama. It stands 32 metres tall, its gaze directed down the valley, visible from kilometers away. The monastery complex itself is worth at least an hour. Go in the early morning when monks are doing prayers, or late afternoon when the light is best for photography.

The views from the monastery roof — out over Diskit village, across the valley to the white peaks — are among the finest in all of Ladakh.

### Turtuk Village

Turtuk is 80 kilometres from Hunder along a rough but manageable road, and it is entirely unlike anywhere else in Ladakh.

The village only opened to tourists in 2009. Before 1971, it was part of Pakistani-administered territory, handed to India during the Kargil conflict. The people here are Balti Muslims, ethnically Tibetan but religiously Islamic, speaking a language related to ancient Tibetan. Walking through Turtuk feels like crossing a cultural boundary — the architecture changes, the food changes, the way people greet you changes.

The village is famous for its apricots and apples. In summer, the orchards are extraordinary. You can buy dried apricots directly from families. There is a small village museum run by a local family that traces the history of Baltistan and the 1971 partition. It is genuinely moving.

Allow half a day minimum. If you can spend a night in one of Turtuk's basic guesthouses, do. The early morning light on the Karakoram peaks from here is unforgettable.

## Where to Stay in Nubra Valley

Most travellers base themselves in Hunder or Diskit. Both have a good range of options.

**Hunder** has the sand dunes and is the more atmospheric base. There are several well-run camps and guesthouses along the main road. Mid-range tented camps (with proper beds, attached bathrooms, and meals) run ₹2,500–4,500 per night. Some of the better-known options include Nubra Organic Retreat and the various camps clustered near the dunes. Book ahead between July and August — Nubra fills up.

**Diskit** is more of a working town with a wider range of budget guesthouses. If you're watching costs, this is the smarter base. Expect to pay ₹800–1,500 per night for a basic room with meals included.

Electricity in both places can be unreliable. Carry a power bank. Nights in Nubra are cold even in July — pack a warm layer.

## A Two-Night Nubra Itinerary

**Day 1 (Arrival Day)**
Leave Leh by 7am. Stop at the South Pullu checkpoint to show your ILP. Drive up to Khardung La — photograph, tea, move on. Descend into Nubra. Check in at Hunder by midday. Rest. Eat lunch. Walk to the dunes in the late afternoon for the golden light and the camels. Dinner at your camp.

**Day 2 (Full Day in Nubra)**
Early morning: Diskit Monastery before the tourist coaches arrive. Spend an hour in the complex. Head back to Hunder for breakfast. Drive to Turtuk — roughly two hours each way on the Shyok River road. Explore the village. Walk through the apricot orchards. Have a simple lunch at a local family's home. Return to Hunder by evening. The drive back along the Shyok at sunset is spectacular.

**Day 3 (Departure)**
Leave by 8am to cross Khardung La comfortably. Back in Leh by early afternoon. Or — if your itinerary allows — continue directly to Pangong Lake via the alternate route through Agham, avoiding Leh entirely (check road conditions and timing with your driver).

## A Few Things No One Tells You

The road to Turtuk is unpaved and can be slow. Budget two hours each way, not 90 minutes.

Mobile connectivity in Nubra is limited. BSNL SIM cards work in Diskit and sometimes Hunder. Other operators drop out after Khardung La. Download your offline maps in Leh.

The sand dunes are most magical at the shoulder hours — sunrise and sunset. The middle of the day, with tour groups arriving and bright flat light, is the worst time to visit. Plan around this.

If you're crossing Khardung La coming back to Leh, start before 9am if possible. By mid-morning the road can get busy with oncoming traffic and the windchill at the summit becomes punishing.

Finally, eat the food. Nubra apricots, Balti flatbread in Turtuk, the simple dal-rice served at roadside dhabas — this is part of what makes the valley worth the effort.

## Your Nubra Valley Trip Starts Here

Nubra Valley rewards the traveller who slows down. Two nights is the minimum. Three nights gives you the space to visit Turtuk properly, spend a morning at the monastery without rushing, and sit with a cup of tea watching the sun move across the dunes.

It is one of the most improbable landscapes on earth — and one of the most beautiful.
