# A Guide to Pangong Lake | Ladakh's Iconic Himalayan Mirror

The photographs don't prepare you for it.

You've seen Pangong Lake in films — the final scene of *3 Idiots*, the endless blue of *Jab Tak Hai Jaan*. You've scrolled past it a hundred times on travel Instagram. You think you know what to expect. Then you crest the final ridge above Spangmik, the lake comes into view, and every previous image you held of it dissolves.

It is not just blue. It shifts — turquoise to cobalt to slate to violet — sometimes within minutes, as cloud shadows move across the surface and the sun angle changes. At 4,320 metres above sea level, with nothing between you and the horizon except cold air and Tibetan sky, it feels less like a lake and more like a rupture in the earth through which the sky has seeped in.

This is one of the world's most extraordinary bodies of water. Here's how to see it properly.

## What Makes Pangong Lake Extraordinary

The facts alone are staggering. Pangong Tso — "tso" means lake in Tibetan — stretches 134 kilometres from west to east, making it one of the longest high-altitude lakes in Asia. At its widest, it is only five kilometres across. The lake sits entirely above 4,200 metres. It is, depending on the source, the highest saltwater lake in the world.

Only 25 percent of the lake lies in India. The remaining 75 percent extends into Tibet, inside Chinese-administered territory. The India–China Line of Actual Control runs through the water at the lake's eastern end, near the Finger Hills — a series of rock spurs that jut into the lake and became the focus of the 2020 military standoff. The Finger 4 area is now the eastern limit for tourists.

None of this diminishes the experience. If anything, the geopolitics add a layer of gravity to what is already a profoundly affecting place.

The colour of the water changes with light and season. In summer, it runs a brilliant turquoise. In winter, it freezes solid — local nomads drive across it. At dawn, when the sun first hits the Himalayan peaks on the far southern shore and the water goes orange and pink, it is one of the most beautiful things most people will see in their lives.

## How to Get There from Leh

There are two routes from Leh to Pangong Lake, and your driver will recommend one or the other depending on conditions.

**Via Chang La Pass** is the traditional route. Chang La sits at 5,425 metres — one of the three highest motorable passes in the world. The road goes from Leh through Karu (37km), then climbs to the pass before descending to the village of Tangste and continuing to the lake. Total distance: roughly 150 kilometres. Driving time: five hours minimum, often more.

**Via Karu (direct, lower altitude)** is the newer option, cutting through the Indus Valley and approaching the lake from the south without going over Chang La. This route is shorter in time — around four hours — and gentler on altitude-anxious travellers.

Both routes are covered in the ILP. Ask your driver which is currently in better condition when you're in Leh.

Depart by 7am either way. The drives are long and you want to arrive with daylight to spare.

## The Permit You Need

All visitors to Pangong Lake require an Inner Line Permit. This applies to Indian nationals and foreign tourists alike.

Apply at the Deputy Commissioner's Office in Leh (Fort Road area, near the main market), or use the LAHDC online portal before you travel. Bring your passport, visa (for foreign nationals), and passport-sized photographs. The cost is ₹400 environment fee plus ₹100 Red Cross fee, with small daily charges.

If you're also planning to visit Nubra Valley on the same trip, get both permits at the same time. The process takes about an hour.

The permit is checked at the checkpoint at Upshi on the Manali highway junction, and again at Tang Tse. Don't forget it.

## Day Trip or Overnight? Stay the Night

This question gets asked constantly. The answer is clear: stay at least one night.

The journey from Leh is five hours each way. A day trip gives you, at best, two to three hours at the lake. You arrive fatigued from the drive, take photographs in mid-afternoon light (the worst time — flat, harsh, uninteresting), and turn straight around.

The people who camp overnight see something different.

At sunset, the mountains turn apricot and the lake turns a deep, bruised violet. After dark, at 4,320 metres with zero light pollution, the stars are overwhelming — the Milky Way visible as a physical presence, not just a suggestion. And then dawn. That is the payoff.

At first light, before the tour coaches arrive from Leh, the lake is quiet and the light does things to the water that no photograph fully captures. You walk to the shore in the cold, breath misting, and the surface is a perfect mirror. This is the Pangong that stays with you.

## Where to Stay: Spangmik Camps

The main accommodation area is Spangmik, seven kilometres from the western tip of the lake on the southern shore. This is where the tented camps cluster, and where most travellers spend the night.

The camps range from basic (simple twin beds, shared bathroom, meals included) to surprisingly comfortable (proper beds with duvets, attached bathrooms, sometimes solar-heated water). Prices run from ₹2,000 per night at the lower end to ₹5,000 or more at the premium camps during peak season.

In July and August — peak season — Spangmik books out. This is not an exaggeration. If you're travelling between mid-July and late August, book your camp a minimum of two weeks in advance, preferably more. Several camps can be booked through tour operators or via phone. Your Leh guesthouse owner will usually know which camps have availability.

A few worth knowing by name: Pangong Retreat Camp, Pangong Eco Camps, and Camp Watermark are among the more established options with consistent reviews. Expect simple, warm, and functional — Pangong is not the place for luxury.

Pack warmer than you think you'll need. Even in summer, nights at Spangmik drop to 5°C or below. A good sleeping bag liner and a fleece layer are essential.

## What to See Around the Lake

### Merak and Man Villages

A few kilometres east of Spangmik, the small villages of Merak and Man sit right on the lakeshore. These are traditional Changpa nomad settlements, and walking through them in the morning gives you a sense of how people actually live beside this lake — not just how it looks on a travel poster.

The shore here is quieter than Spangmik, and the views east along the lake are extraordinary.

### The India–China Border Marker

The limit for tourists is now Finger 4 — a rocky spur that juts into the lake — following new restrictions after the 2020 standoff. The drive from Spangmik to Finger 4 takes about 30 minutes on a rough track. The border itself is not visible, but the desolation increases as you head east, and the sense of being at the edge of something — geographically, politically, psychologically — is real.

### The Lake Shore Walk

One of the best things to do at Pangong costs nothing. Wake at 5:30am. Walk the shore while it's still quiet. The light at that hour, the sound of the water, the cold air — this is the experience.

## Practical Information

**Connectivity:** Mobile data is unreliable at the lake. BSNL cards get signal in Spangmik sometimes. Don't count on it. Download maps offline in Leh.

**ATMs:** There are none. Carry enough cash for your stay before you leave Leh. Camps do not take cards.

**Altitude:** At 4,320 metres, Pangong is higher than Nubra Valley but lower than Khardung La. If you've been in Leh for three-plus days and feel well, you should be fine. Still, walk slowly on arrival, drink water, and skip the evening run.

**Photography:** Bring a wide-angle lens if you shoot on a mirrorless or DSLR. The lake is too vast for a standard lens to do it justice. For phone photographers, shoot in the morning and evening — midday light is flat and the lake loses its colour drama.

**Drones:** Requires special permission near the LAC. Check current regulations with the DC office in Leh before attempting to fly.

## The Moment the Lake Gives You

Every traveller to Pangong has a moment. For most people, it is dawn on the second day — before breakfast, before the coaches arrive, before another person appears on that shore.

The lake is completely still. The sky is going from black to charcoal to a pale, burning gold. The peaks across the water catch the first light. The water mirrors everything.

You've seen this place in films. You've seen it in photographs. But you have never seen it like this — alone, cold, altitude making your breath a little shallow, standing at the edge of a body of water that stretches 134 kilometres eastward into Tibet.

This is why you came to Ladakh.
