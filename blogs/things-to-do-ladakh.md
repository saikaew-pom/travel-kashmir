# 15 Wonderful Things To Do in Ladakh | Beyond the Monasteries

Most travellers arrive with one image fixed in their mind: Pangong Lake glittering blue against brown mountains. It's a worthy goal. But Ladakh is far more than that one frame.

At 3,500 metres above sea level, Leh sits inside a high-altitude desert that holds more than 100 monasteries, some of the world's highest motorable roads, a river confluence unlike anything else on earth, and villages that have remained largely unchanged for centuries. Two weeks here still won't exhaust it.

This guide gives you the full picture — 15 specific things worth doing, from the iconic to the ones most tourists miss entirely.

## Landscapes & Natural Wonders

**1. Watch the Colour Shift at Pangong Lake at Dawn**
Pangong Lake sits at 4,320 metres and stretches 134 kilometres into Tibet, with roughly a third of it in India. Arrive before 6am and you'll have the shore almost to yourself. The water moves through turquoise, sapphire, and silver within a single morning — each shift more disorienting than the last. Camp here overnight or take the 5-hour drive from Leh and stay at a lakeside tent camp. The crowds arrive by 9am; be there before them.

**2. Cross Khardung La — One of the World's Highest Motorable Roads**
Khardung La passes at 5,600 metres, 39 kilometres north of Leh. The road is your gateway into Nubra Valley, but the pass itself is worth the stop. Altitude hits hard here — get out slowly, take photographs, don't sprint. The views down into the valleys on both sides are staggering: a landscape so stripped of everything soft it looks like another planet. Most vehicles do it in a shared jeep; the full Leh-to-Nubra drive takes around 2.5 hours.

**3. Stand at the Sangam Viewpoint**
Near Nimmu, about 35 kilometres west of Leh, two rivers collide. The turquoise Zanskar meets the silty brown Indus, and for several hundred metres the two colours run side by side without mixing — a clean line where two worlds hold their boundary. It's one of the most visually striking natural phenomena in all of Ladakh and costs nothing to see. Stop here on your way to Alchi or Lamayuru.

**4. Watch Magnetic Hill Do Its Thing**
Thirty kilometres from Leh on the Leh–Kargil highway, a painted box on the road marks the spot. Put your vehicle in neutral. It appears to roll uphill. The effect is a gravitational illusion caused by the surrounding terrain, but knowing the science doesn't make it less strange. It takes five minutes and makes for an excellent conversation starter. The Indus River and Zanskar mountains frame the scene beautifully while you're there.

## Culture & Monasteries

**5. Attend Morning Puja at Thiksey Monastery**
Thiksey Monastery sits 19 kilometres east of Leh, rising 12 storeys up a hillside in a style modelled after the Potala Palace in Lhasa. Arrive at 6am for morning puja — monks in deep red robes chanting in candlelit halls, butter lamps flickering, the smell of juniper incense in cold air. This is one of the most affecting spiritual experiences in Ladakh. Entry is ₹50, and respectful dress — covered shoulders and knees — is required.

**6. Visit Hemis Monastery During the Hemis Festival**
Hemis is Ladakh's largest and wealthiest monastery, 45 kilometres from Leh and set in a hidden valley that feels deliberately secreted away. In June or July (the exact date shifts with the Tibetan lunar calendar), the Hemis Festival brings monks into the courtyard for Tsechu — masked dance performances depicting Buddhist teachings that have been performed here for centuries. Book your accommodation months ahead if you're targeting the festival. Hemis is also the primary research site for snow leopard conservation in the region.

**7. Climb Leh Palace in the Morning**
Leh Palace was built in 1630, nine storeys of Tibetan-style fortress architecture perched above the old city — modelled after the Potala Palace in Tibet and predating it. Go early, before the tour groups arrive around 10am. The interior is partly ruined and atmospheric: carved wooden beams, collapsed floors, small chapels still holding faded murals. Entry is ₹15 for Indians, ₹100 for foreign tourists. The views from the roof stretch across the entire Indus Valley.

**8. Walk Through Diskit Monastery in Nubra Valley**
Established in 1963, Diskit is the oldest monastery in Nubra Valley. It looks over the valley from a ridge, and a giant Maitreya Buddha statue — added in 2010 — gazes serenely toward Pakistan. The monastery itself is more intimate than Thiksey or Hemis, with fewer tourists and monks who are often willing to talk. Combine it with the sand dunes at Hunder, a short drive away.

## Adventure

**9. Ride a Bactrian Camel on the Nubra Sand Dunes**
The Silver Sand Dunes at Hunder in Nubra Valley are a geographical anomaly — desert dunes sitting beneath snow-capped peaks. Bactrian camels, the two-humped variety, are available to ride here for ₹300 for a 20-minute circuit. It's a short ride but an absurd and wonderful one: you are simultaneously in the Himalayas and the Sahara. The dunes are best visited in the late afternoon when the light turns amber and the crowds thin.

**10. Trek the Markha Valley**
The Markha Valley Trek is one of Ladakh's finest multi-day routes — typically 8 to 9 days, covering approximately 80 kilometres through remote villages, high passes, and dramatic canyon landscapes. You cross Kongmaru La at 5,250 metres and walk through settlements where children wave from doorways and farmers still harvest barley by hand. No technical climbing skill is required, but good fitness and proper acclimatisation are essential. Most trekkers hire a local guide through agencies in Leh.

**11. Consider the Chadar Trek (January–February Only)**
The Chadar Trek follows the frozen Zanskar River in the depths of winter — walking on ice through sheer gorges that are completely inaccessible by road. It runs for roughly 105 kilometres over 9–10 days and is one of the most demanding treks in India: temperatures drop to -25°C, and you sleep in shallow caves carved into the cliff face. Not for everyone, but those who do it describe it as unlike anything else. The window is January and February only, when the river freezes solidly enough to walk on.

## Hidden Gems & Slow Travel

**12. Spend a Night in a Village Homestay**
The highways of Ladakh are well-trodden. The villages just off them are not. A night in a Ladakhi homestay — typically ₹800 to ₹1,500 per night including meals — puts you inside a family home built around a central kitchen, sleeping under thick blankets in a low-ceilinged room, eating Skyu (a thick pasta stew with root vegetables) or Thukpa (noodle soup) that came off a wood-fire stove. Your host likely speaks limited English; the communication gap matters less than you'd think.

**13. Sleep Under Ladakh's Night Sky**
At 3,500 metres, with no industrial light pollution for hundreds of kilometres, Ladakh offers some of the clearest night skies accessible by road anywhere on earth. The Milky Way is not a faint suggestion here — it's a dense, luminous band overhead. The best spots are around Pangong Lake, Nubra Valley, or any village far from Leh. Bring a warm sleeping bag; temperatures at night drop sharply even in summer.

**14. Walk to Shanti Stupa at Sunset**
Shanti Stupa sits on Chanspa Hill above Leh, built in 1991 by Japanese Buddhist monk Gyomyo Nakamura in the interest of world peace. It offers 360-degree panoramic views of Leh town, the Indus Valley, and the surrounding peaks. Sunrise and sunset are both excellent times to visit — the stupa turns gold in the low light, and the prayer flags crack in the wind above the valley. A steep 500-step staircase leads up from Changspa Road; the walk takes about 20 minutes.

**15. Drive to Turtuk Village**
Turtuk sits 205 kilometres from Leh, near the Line of Control with Pakistan, and was only opened to tourists in 2010. It's one of the few Balti-culture villages accessible in India — the architecture, language, and food are distinct from the Tibetan-Buddhist Ladakh further east. The village is terraced into a narrow gorge of the Shyok River, surrounded by apricot trees that bloom brilliantly in April. The drive alone — through Nubra, past Diskit and Hunder — is worth the day.

## Before You Go

Ladakh rewards travellers who slow down. The 48-hour acclimatisation period when you first arrive in Leh is not optional — altitude sickness at 3,500 metres is real, and your second day here will be more enjoyable than your first. Use those early days for Leh town, palace visits, and the bazaar. Save the high passes and long drives for days three and four onwards.

The best months are June through September. Roads to Pangong and Nubra are typically open from May to October, weather permitting. Khardung La can close after heavy snowfall even in summer.
