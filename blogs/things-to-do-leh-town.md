# 10 Things To Do in Leh Town | Markets, Palaces & Mountain Views

You land in Leh and the altitude hits you before your luggage does. A dull headache, a strange tiredness in your legs, an odd lightness in your chest. The elevation here is 3,500 metres — higher than any point in the Alps — and your body needs two full days before it's ready for the passes and lakes beyond town.

Every experienced Ladakh traveller will tell you the same thing: do not fight the acclimatisation. It exists for a reason. Skipping it means nausea, sleeplessness, or worse, at a mountain pass 2,000 metres higher than where you're standing now.

Here's what most people miss: Leh itself is extraordinary. Two days in town, done right, will give you more than enough — a 400-year-old palace, one of the finest sunset viewpoints in the Himalayas, a market that comes alive after dark, a museum run by the Indian Army, and some of the best Tibetan food outside Tibet. You don't need to rush out to the lakes yet. Stay, look around, and let Leh earn your attention.

## Day One: The Old City & The Heights

**1. Leh Palace — Go Early, Before the Crowds**
Leh Palace was built in 1630 by King Sengge Namgyal, rising nine storeys above the old city in a Tibetan-style fortress architecture directly modelled on the Potala Palace in Tibet — and constructed 12 years before work on the Potala began. It is one of the oldest buildings in Ladakh and one of the most impressive in all of the Himalayas.

Go early — by 8am if you can. The entrance ticket is ₹15 for Indian nationals and ₹100 for foreign visitors. The interior is a mix of preserved and ruined: collapsed wooden floors, narrow passageways, small chapels holding faded murals, carved lintels blackened by centuries of butter-lamp smoke. The Archaeological Survey of India has partially restored several rooms, but much of the palace retains its raw, half-abandoned quality. The rooftop views over Leh town and the Indus Valley are the best you'll get from anywhere in the old city.

**2. Namgyal Tsemo Gompa — Above the Palace**
Directly above Leh Palace, perched at the very summit of the ridge, Namgyal Tsemo Gompa is the small monastery that has watched over Leh since the 15th century. The climb from the palace rooftop takes another 15–20 minutes and involves a rough path up loose stone. It's worth every step. The monastery holds a three-storey Maitreya Buddha statue painted in gold, and the prayer flags at the summit snap in near-constant wind. From here, you can see the full geometry of Leh below — the old city, the bazaar, the modern town spreading south, the military cantonment further out, the river valley beyond.

**3. Shanti Stupa at Sunset (or Sunrise)**
Shanti Stupa sits on Chanspa Hill, built in 1991 by Japanese Buddhist organisation Nipponzan-Myohoji to promote world peace and commemorate 2,500 years of Buddhism. Climb the 500 steps from Changspa Road (about 20 minutes at a slow acclimatisation pace) or take an auto-rickshaw to the base for ₹50–80 from Leh Market.

The stupa itself is white and pristine, inlaid with gold-painted reliefs depicting scenes from the life of the Buddha. But what brings people back is the view: 360 degrees of Leh, the Indus Valley, and the mountain ranges stacked beyond. Sunrise paints everything amber; sunset turns the stupa gold and the mountains behind it purple. Either works. Sunset draws slightly more visitors; sunrise is quieter and colder.

**4. Jama Masjid — The Old City's Other Heart**
Leh's Jama Masjid sits in the centre of the old city, built in the 17th century by Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb as part of an agreement with the Ladakhi kingdom. It's a striking building: a mosque with multiple minarets and a bright yellow-painted facade, sitting directly below the Buddhist palace and monastery on the ridge above. The architectural coexistence — mosque at the base, Buddhist palace above — reflects Ladakh's long history of religious pluralism. The mosque is active and visitors are welcome during non-prayer hours; dress modestly and remove shoes.

**5. Leh Main Bazaar — Come in the Evening**
Leh's main market runs along the central bazaar road and is best experienced after 6pm, when the heat of the day drops and the street fills with locals, pilgrims, trekkers, and traders. Stalls sell pashmina shawls, Thangka paintings (allow 15 minutes to look properly at a good one), prayer wheels, carved singing bowls, dried apricots and walnuts from Nubra, and Ladakhi jewellery. Quality varies enormously — take your time, compare prices between stalls, and don't buy the first thing that catches your eye.

The best local food in town is found along and just off the bazaar: momos (steamed or fried dumplings, ₹80–150 per plate), Thukpa (thick Tibetan noodle soup, ₹100–150), and butter tea — Po Cha — which is salty and made with yak butter. It's an acquired taste. Try it once. Restaurants worth finding include The Tibetan Kitchen (two minutes from the main road, always busy), Bon Appetit, and Penguin Garden.

## Day Two: Museums, Markets & Easy Drives

**6. Tibetan Refugee Market**
A short walk from Leh Main Bazaar, the Tibetan Refugee Market is a covered arcade of small shops run by Tibetan families who settled in Ladakh after 1959. The goods here tend toward higher quality and better craftsmanship than the tourist-facing stalls: hand-knotted carpets, fine pashmina, Tibetan antiques, hand-painted prayerboxes. The sellers are often willing to explain the significance of what they're selling — the symbolism of a Thangka, the traditional use of a particular weaving pattern — which makes the shopping more interesting. Bargaining is expected but not aggressive.

**7. Leh Friday Market — Local Produce and Real Life**
Every Friday morning, farmers from villages around Leh bring produce into town: fresh vegetables, dried fruit, herbs, locally made butter, yak cheese called Chhurpe (hard, salty, and extraordinary), and medicinal plants. The market is on a small square near the main bazaar and runs from around 8am until mid-morning. It's less curated than the tourist markets and more genuinely local. Buy dried apricots from Nubra — they're better than anything you'll find in a packet back home — and a wedge of Chhurpe if you want something to chew on the road.

**8. Hall of Fame Museum — Indian Army's Ladakh Story**
Eight kilometres from Leh town on the road toward Khardung La, the Hall of Fame Museum is operated by the Indian Army and dedicated to soldiers who served in the Siachen and Kargil campaigns in the high Himalayas. Entry is ₹200.

The museum is split across multiple halls: displays cover the 1947–48 war, the 1971 conflict, and the 1999 Kargil War in considerable detail, with weaponry, personal effects, maps, and personal stories. One section is dedicated to the extreme conditions of Siachen Glacier — the world's highest battlefield at over 6,000 metres — with displays of the cold-weather equipment that soldiers used to survive. Whatever your views on military museums, this one is sobering and well-presented. Set aside 90 minutes.

**9. Stok Palace Museum — Living History, 15km Out**
Stok Palace is 15 kilometres south of Leh, a small drive along the east bank of the Indus. Unlike Leh Palace, which is partially ruined and managed by the Archaeological Survey, Stok is still a functioning royal residence — the Ladakhi royal family actually lives here. The museum section (entry ₹50) holds royal regalia, ancient thangka paintings, turquoise and coral jewellery, ceremonial armour, and portraits of the Ladakhi kings. The collection is personal and curated with obvious care. The palace also operates as a heritage hotel if you want to stay — one of the more unusual accommodation options in Ladakh.

The short drive south also takes you through the village of Choglamsar and past several smaller monasteries. The Stok Kangri mountain (6,153m), visible throughout the drive, is one of the most popular high-altitude trekking peaks in Ladakh.

**10. Eat a Proper Ladakhi Meal**
Before you leave Leh for the passes and lakes, sit down for a proper meal. Not street food, not the vague "Himalayan buffet" that some guesthouses serve. Order Skyu — a thick, slow-cooked stew of hand-rolled pasta and root vegetables that the Ladakhi people have eaten through winter for centuries. Order Butter Tea at least once. Try the yak meat momos if you're not vegetarian. Ask for a plate of Chhurpe on the side.

The Tibetan Kitchen on Fort Road is the most consistent option for Ladakhi and Tibetan food at a good price (main courses ₹150–300). Bon Appetit near the bazaar does better Continental food but also a good Thukpa. Penguin Garden is a popular meeting spot with an open courtyard — go for the atmosphere and the apple tea.

Two days in Leh. Eight acclimatising walks between these sites. Evenings in the bazaar. Meals that taste exactly like nowhere else. Your body adjusts, your eye adjusts, and by day three you're ready for Pangong.

## The Slow Start Is the Right Start

Ladakh asks something from you that most destinations don't: patience. The altitude demands it. And the places that reward a slower pace — the old city lanes, the palace at first light, the monastery above, the rooftop of a guesthouse watching Orion rise over the Stok range — these are the memories that stay.

Two days in Leh is not a compromise. It's where the trip begins.
