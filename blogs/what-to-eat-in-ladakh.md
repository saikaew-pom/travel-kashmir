# What To Eat in Ladakh | A Guide to Thukpa, Momos, Butter Tea & Local Favourites

Most travellers arrive in Ladakh thinking about mountains and monasteries. They leave thinking about the food.

Not because Ladakhi cuisine is glamorous — it isn't, by the standards of Bangkok or Beirut. It is the food of a high-altitude people living through winters of –25°C on the roof of the world: hearty, warming, deeply nourishing, and built around whatever grows short-season at 3,500 metres. Barley, root vegetables, yak milk, dried apricots, and wild herbs.

But within those honest constraints lies some of the most comforting food in Asia. A bowl of Thukpa at 4,000 metres when the wind is picking up outside. A plate of Momos eaten beside a kerosene stove while snow falls on the prayer flags. Butter Tea — salty, not sweet, served from a thermos — pressed into your hands by a Ladakhi grandmother who doesn't speak your language but knows you're cold.

This is your guide to eating in Ladakh — the essential dishes, where to find them, what to buy to take home, and the restaurants in Leh worth seeking out.

## The 7 Dishes to Eat Before You Leave Ladakh

### 1. Thukpa — The Everyday Bowl

Thukpa is Ladakh's answer to everything: cold mornings, long drives, altitude headaches, and days when you need something warm and sustaining. It is a thick noodle soup — Tibetan in origin — made with hand-rolled flat noodles, vegetables (spinach, turnip, carrots), and often chicken or mutton in the non-vegetarian version. The broth is flavoured with ginger, garlic, and dried herbs, and it arrives in a bowl large enough to feel genuinely restorative.

You will find Thukpa on every menu in every restaurant from Leh to the Pangong tented camps. It is rarely expensive (₹80–200 depending on location) and rarely bad. The best versions — thick broth, handmade noodles, generous vegetables — are found at family-run dhabas in Leh's Old Town rather than the tourist-facing restaurants on Fort Road.

### 2. Momos — The Crowd Pleaser

Momos are the most universally loved food across Ladakh, and for good reason. These steamed dumplings — filled with finely minced vegetables, cheese, or meat — arrive in a bamboo steamer basket, accompanied by a small bowl of hot chilli sauce made with Ladakhi red chilli and tomato. The steamed version is more traditional; the fried version (slightly crisp on the outside, gooey within) has become increasingly popular in Leh's newer restaurants.

A plate of 8 steamed Momos costs ₹80–150 at a local dhaba. At tourist-facing restaurants on Fort Road, the same dish might cost ₹150–250 — still not expensive, but the local dhaba version is more authentic and usually better.

### 3. Po Cha — Butter Tea

**Po Cha** is the drink of Ladakh, and it will polarise you immediately. It is not what you expect from a tea — it is not sweet, not milky in any familiar sense, and not subtle. It is made by churning strong black tea with yak butter and salt for several minutes until it becomes a rich, emulsified, savory drink with a faintly cheesy back note.

At altitude, it makes complete physiological sense. The fat provides sustained energy, the salt replaces electrolytes lost through altitude-driven exertion and urination, and the heat warms you from the inside. Local Ladakhis drink it constantly — from thermoses, from clay cups passed around shared tables, from the kerosene stoves in roadside shacks at 5,000 metres.

Drink at least one cup. It will not taste like anything else you have ever drunk. It may grow on you. It will certainly fuel you.

### 4. Skyu — The Pasta Stew You Didn't Know You Needed

Skyu is Ladakh's most traditional dish and the one least likely to appear on tourist menus. It is a thick, peasant-style stew of hand-rolled, thumb-pressed pasta dough cooked slowly with root vegetables — turnips, radishes, carrots — and seasoned with dried herbs and sometimes dried apricots, which add a subtle sweetness that balances the earthiness of the vegetables.

It is the kind of dish that was designed to be eaten in winter by people who had been outside in the cold all day. It is dense, warming, and deeply satisfying in a way that is entirely unlike restaurant food. The best Skyu in Leh is found in homestays where the host family still makes it in the traditional way — cooked slowly in a pot on the stove, served ladled into deep bowls with a piece of bread.

Ask your guesthouse host if they make Skyu. If they do, order it.

### 5. Chhurpe — Dried Yak Cheese

Chhurpe is dried yak cheese — sun-dried, very hard, and intensely savoury. The texture when you first bite into it is somewhere between a very old cheddar and a piece of wood. Chew it slowly and the flavour develops: nutty, lactic, slightly sharp.

It is sold in Leh's Main Bazaar and the Tibetan Market in small rounds or rectangles, hanging in clusters from market stalls. Local Ladakhis eat it as a snack throughout the day — and it makes an excellent trail snack if you're trekking, providing protein and fat in a lightweight, unrefrigerated form.

**Buy some to take home** — it is one of the most distinctive food souvenirs from Ladakh, travels well without refrigeration, and keeps for weeks.

### 6. Ladakhi Pulao — Festive Rice

Ladakhi Pulao is fragrant basmati rice cooked with dried fruits — apricots, raisins — and nuts, seasoned with cardamom and saffron. It appears at festivals, celebrations, and in the better homestays as a weekend special. It reflects the Central Asian influence on Ladakhi cuisine — the dried fruit, the aromatic rice, the generous hand with spice — and is noticeably different from the plainer Pulao found in the Indian plains below.

If you encounter it on a menu or in a homestay, order it.

### 7. Tsampa — Barley Flour Porridge

Tsampa is the staple that has sustained Tibetan and Ladakhi populations for centuries: roasted barley flour mixed with butter tea to form a dough or porridge. It is calorie-dense, nutrient-rich, and extraordinarily simple. You won't find it on restaurant menus, but in homestays and remote village guesthouses — particularly in Nubra Valley and Zanskar — it may appear at breakfast. Eat it. It is the most honest thing you will taste in Ladakh.

## The Food of Nubra Valley — Apricots & Walnuts

Nubra Valley at 3,000–3,100 metres is lower and warmer than Leh, and its microclimate supports fruit growing. The valley is famous for its **apricots** — harvested in July and August, they are smaller, more intensely flavoured, and sweeter than most apricots sold in supermarkets. Fresh in season, they are extraordinary. Dried — sun-dried on rooftops throughout August — they are a different product entirely: concentrated, chewy, deeply sweet, with a tartness that fresh apricots don't have.

Buy a bag of Nubra dried apricots at the village market in Hunder or Turtuk. They keep for months and are among the best food souvenirs Ladakh produces.

Nubra also produces **walnuts**, **sea buckthorn berries** (tart, orange, extremely high in Vitamin C — often made into a local juice), and in Turtuk, small sweet **apples** and **rose hip jam**. Turtuk's apple harvest in September is worth timing a trip around.

## Where to Eat in Leh Town

**The Tibetan Kitchen** (Old Town): One of the best places in Leh for authentic Tibetan and Ladakhi food. Thukpa, Momos, Skyu, and Tsampa all on the menu. Small, simple, and usually packed with local Ladakhis — a reliable indicator of quality. Mains ₹150–300.

**Bon Appetit** (near Shanti Stupa road): Rooftop restaurant with the best views in Leh. Good for a mix of Ladakhi, Tibetan, and Continental food. The Momos and Thukpa are solid; the rooftop makes any meal worth having. Mains ₹200–450.

**Penguin Garden Restaurant** (Fort Road): A long-running Leh institution popular with travellers. Reliable, wide-ranging menu, good for groups with mixed dietary preferences. Mains ₹180–400.

**Lamayuru Restaurant** (Main Bazaar): Simple, clean, friendly. Good Thukpa and a useful stop for a quick lunch near the bazaar. Mains ₹120–250.

**Local Dhabas (Old Town)**: Unnamed but essential — the small, plastic-table restaurants run by local families in the Old Town serve the most authentic food at the most honest prices. Follow the Ladakhi locals at lunchtime. You'll find the best Skyu and Thukpa in Leh this way.

## What to Bring Home from Ladakh

Ladakh's food souvenirs travel well and tell a genuine story about the region:

- **Dried apricots from Nubra Valley**: The best food souvenir from Ladakh. Buy directly from market stalls in Hunder or Turtuk for the freshest product.
- **Chhurpe (dried yak cheese)**: Keeps without refrigeration, unusual, and genuinely good. Found at Leh Main Bazaar.
- **Ladakhi honey**: Produced from the wildflower meadows around Leh and Nubra. A limited product — buy it when you see it.
- **Sea buckthorn products**: Juice, jam, and dried berries. Available in Leh's health stores and some guesthouses. High in Vitamin C, distinctly Ladakhi.
- **Kashmiri saffron**: Not technically Ladakhi (it comes from the Kashmir Valley below), but sold widely in Leh's bazaar at prices far lower than you'd find elsewhere. The Pampore region of Kashmir is one of the world's finest saffron-producing areas. Buy from a reputable shop and check it's genuine — look for deep red threads with a slightly orange tip and a strong floral scent.

## Eat Like the Mountains Deserve It

Ladakhi food is not flashy. It won't win international awards or feature in fine dining guides. But eaten in context — at altitude, after hours on a mountain road, in the company of warm hosts who grew or raised everything on the table — it is some of the most satisfying food you will eat anywhere.

Slow down. Order the Skyu even if Thukpa is safer. Drink the butter tea. Buy the apricots in Nubra rather than waiting for the airport gift shop. The food is part of the journey.
