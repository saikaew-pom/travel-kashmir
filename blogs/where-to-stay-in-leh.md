# Where To Stay in Leh Ladakh | Guesthouses, Heritage Hotels & Pangong Camps

The first decision most Ladakh travellers agonise over is the itinerary. The second — and one that matters just as much — is the accommodation. Get it wrong and you spend your rest days in a thin-walled room with no hot water, waking up to a view of a concrete courtyard. Get it right and you fall asleep to the sound of wind in the prayer flags, with the Stok Kangri glacier visible from your bed.

Ladakh's accommodation ranges from family-run homestays that cost ₹800 a night to heritage palace hotels that cost ₹18,000. There are glamping camps at Pangong Lake where the nights get close to freezing even in August, and sun-warmed guesthouses in Leh's Old Town where the hosts bring you butter tea at 6am without being asked.

This guide walks you through the options — neighbourhood by neighbourhood in Leh, then out to the overnight stops at Nubra and Pangong — so you can choose what's right for your style and budget.

## Where to Stay in Leh — The Neighbourhoods

Leh is small and manageable. The town centre is compact enough that most neighbourhoods are within a 10–20 minute walk of the Main Bazaar. Your choice of neighbourhood shapes the character of your stay more than the specific property.

### Old Town (Leh Khar)
The Old Town, clustered below Leh Palace, is the most atmospheric part of Leh — narrow lanes, whitewashed houses, prayer wheels set into stone walls, and the sense that you are staying inside history rather than beside it. Most accommodation here is small guesthouses and homestays, often family-run.

Expect: traditional architecture, basic but clean rooms, shared bathrooms in budget properties, home-cooked meals, and the sound of monks chanting from the nearby Namgyal Tsemo Gompa at dawn.

**Best for:** Solo travellers, cultural immersion seekers, those who want Leh to feel lived-in rather than touristy.

### Chanspa
Chanspa sits northwest of the Main Bazaar, slightly elevated, with the better guesthouses offering views toward the Shanti Stupa. Quieter than the bazaar area, with more greenery — apricot and poplar trees line the lanes in summer. Good concentration of mid-range guesthouses and a few boutique properties.

**Best for:** Couples and small groups wanting a calmer base without sacrificing convenience.

### Main Bazaar & Fort Road
The most central and convenient neighbourhood, with the highest density of restaurants, cafés, travel agencies, and shops. The hotels here tend toward the functional rather than the atmospheric, but it's hard to beat for sheer ease of access.

**Best for:** First-timers who want everything on their doorstep, travellers on tight itineraries.

## Budget Accommodation (₹800–1,500/night)

### Homestays
The best budget experience in Ladakh is a homestay in a local Ladakhi family's home. They are not luxury — expect squat toilets in some cases, solar-heated water (warm but not always hot), and simple dal-rice meals — but they deliver something no hotel can: a window into daily Ladakhi life.

Hosts are almost uniformly warm, many speak enough English to share stories about the region, and meals are home-cooked with local produce. In summer, breakfast is often eaten on a rooftop with mountain views.

**Budget**: ₹800–1,500/night, usually including breakfast and sometimes dinner.
**Book via:** Booking.com lists some, but the best homestays are found through local tour operators or word of mouth. Ask your tour agency to recommend a family-run property.

### Budget Guesthouses
A step up from homestays — private bathrooms, proper hot water (solar or electric), and sometimes Wi-Fi in the lobby. Leh has dozens of reliable budget guesthouses in Chanspa and the Old Town area. Room quality varies significantly; read recent reviews and check photos carefully.

## Mid-Range Hotels (₹2,500–6,000/night)

At this price point in Leh, you get reliable hot water, a private bathroom, comfortable beds, and usually Wi-Fi. The better mid-range properties add garden courtyards, mountain view rooms, and a quality in-house restaurant.

**Hotel Leh Berry** (Chanspa): Well-regarded for cleanliness and service, garden setting, good mountain views from upper rooms. Around ₹3,000–4,500/night.

**Mentsee Khang Guest House** (Old Town): Small, character-filled property near the Old Town pharmacy district. Traditional architecture, warm hospitality, great value. Around ₹2,500–3,500/night.

**Hotel Omasila** (Fort Road): Popular with returning travellers for consistent quality and a solid in-house restaurant. Central location. Around ₹3,000–5,000/night.

At this tier, book 6–8 weeks ahead for July and August. Good mid-range rooms in Leh fill quickly in peak season.

## Luxury Accommodation (₹8,000–25,000/night)

### The Grand Dragon Leh
The closest thing to a five-star hotel in Ladakh. Located in a quiet part of Upper Leh, The Grand Dragon has well-appointed rooms with heated floors, a spa, a restaurant with good Continental and Indian food, and — crucially for altitude arrivals — an oxygen bar in the lobby. Rooms from ₹10,000–25,000/night depending on season and room type.

**Best for:** Travellers who want to be comfortable at altitude without compromising on quality.

### Stok Palace Heritage Hotel
Fifteen kilometres south of Leh, on the grounds of the Stok Palace — the ancestral home of the Namgyal dynasty, Ladakh's royal family. A handful of heritage rooms and suites are available within the palace complex itself, decorated with antique furniture, traditional Ladakhi textiles, and royal artefacts. The palace museum (open to the public, entry ₹50) gives guests extraordinary access.

Rooms from ₹8,000–18,000/night. The remoteness (15km from Leh) is the one trade-off — but the setting is extraordinary.

### Ladakh Sarai
A boutique luxury tented camp set in a meadow near Thiksey village, 17km from Leh. Stone and canvas structures with proper beds, ensuite bathrooms with heated water, and unobstructed views of the Stok Kangri glacier (6,121m). The camp has a warm communal lounge, excellent food, and the kind of quiet that urban hotels can't replicate.

From ₹12,000–20,000/night including meals. Book well ahead — only a limited number of tents.

## Staying in Nubra Valley

The standard base for Nubra Valley is **Hunder** (for the Sand Dunes) or **Diskit** (closer to the monastery). Options range from simple family guesthouses (₹1,000–2,000/night) to dedicated glamping camps set among the dunes.

**Camp Chunshul** (Hunder) and **Desert Drives Camp** are the better-known options. Glamping tents with beds, linen, and attached bathrooms run ₹3,000–5,500/night including meals. Solar power is the norm — don't expect air conditioning (you won't need it) but do expect the nights to get surprisingly cold even in summer.

Two nights in Nubra is the recommended minimum — enough to visit the dunes, Diskit Monastery, and make the worthwhile trip to Turtuk village.

## Staying at Pangong Lake

The tented camps at **Spangmik** village on the Indian shore of Pangong are the most atmospheric overnight experience in all of Ladakh. They are also the most logistically challenging.

Expect: canvas tents with basic but clean beds and duvets, a shared (or ensuite in better camps) bathroom, solar-only electricity (charge your devices before the sun goes down), and meals cooked over gas stoves — usually a simple but satisfying dinner and breakfast.

**Temperature:** Even in August, Pangong nights drop to 2–5°C. In October, below freezing. Bring your down jacket, thermal layers, and warm socks — you will need them for the pre-dawn walk to the lakeshore for sunrise.

**Price:** ₹2,500–6,000/night including dinner and breakfast. The higher-end camps have en-suite bathrooms and slightly more substantial canvas structures.

**Book 6–8 weeks ahead** for July and August. The camps at Pangong are a finite resource and fill completely in peak season.

> **The Rule:** Never do Pangong as a day trip. The 5-hour drive each way gives you 2 hours at the lake. Stay overnight. The dawn light — when the lake shifts from black to deep indigo to turquoise as the sun clears the ridge — is the experience. Don't skip it.

## What to Book First

In peak season (July–August), prioritise in this order: Pangong camp (fills first, fewest options), Nubra Valley camp (fills second), then your Leh accommodation (more supply, more flexibility). Leaving Leh hotels to the last is usually fine — leaving Pangong camps to the last is a common mistake that forces day-trip compromises.
